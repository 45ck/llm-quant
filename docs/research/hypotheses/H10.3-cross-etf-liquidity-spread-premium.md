## Statement

Three dollar-neutral spread trades — long IWM / short SPY, long EEM /
short EFA, long HYG / short LQD — each express the liquidity premium
within an asset class (small vs large equity, EM vs DM equity, high-yield
vs investment-grade credit). Entering all three spreads when an aggregate
liquidity index signals expansion, and closing when it signals contraction,
generates Sharpe > 0.80 with MaxDD < 15%. The dollar-neutral construction
removes directional market exposure, isolating the liquidity premium.

## Mechanism

The liquidity premium manifests as a SPREAD between more-liquid and
less-liquid assets within the same asset class:

1. **IWM / SPY spread (equity liquidity)**: Small-caps (IWM) have higher
   bid-ask spreads, lower daily volume, and greater price impact per trade
   than large-caps (SPY). Investors demand a liquidity premium for holding
   IWM. During liquidity expansions, this premium compresses (IWM
   outperforms SPY) as capital flows down the liquidity spectrum. During
   contractions, the premium widens (SPY outperforms IWM) as capital
   flees to safety.

2. **EEM / EFA spread (international liquidity)**: Emerging market ETFs
   (EEM) are less liquid than developed market (EFA) due to capital
   controls, thinner order books, and currency convertibility risk. The
   same expansion/contraction dynamic applies: EEM outperforms EFA when
   global liquidity expands.

3. **HYG / LQD spread (credit liquidity)**: High-yield bonds (HYG) are
   less liquid than investment-grade (LQD) — wider bid-ask spreads,
   fewer market-makers, higher transaction costs. The HYG/LQD spread
   tracks credit conditions and liquidity appetite.

4. **Aggregate liquidity index as regime filter**: A composite z-score of
   four inputs — Amihud ratio (inverted), aggregate dollar volume trend,
   VIX level (inverted), and HYG-LQD credit spread — classifies the
   liquidity regime. Entering spreads only during expansion periods avoids
   the catastrophic spread widening that occurs during liquidity crises.

**Why dollar-neutral**: Each spread is approximately beta-neutral to its
asset class (long one ETF, short the same dollar amount of a correlated
ETF). This means the strategy profits from the RELATIVE performance of
less-liquid vs more-liquid assets, not from directional market moves.
The three spreads are themselves partially uncorrelated (equity, EM,
credit), providing diversification within the strategy.

Each of the 3 spreads uses 30% of NAV (15% long, 15% short = 30% gross

## Falsification Criteria

1. None of the 3 spreads shows positive return during identified liquidity-expansion regimes (liquidity premium absent in ETFs)
2. Aggregate liquidity index has no forward predictive power for spread returns (regression p > 0.05)
3. Shuffled liquidity index: spread timing does not beat static hold of all 3 spreads
4. Dollar-neutral construction does NOT reduce drawdown vs directional long-only (market neutrality is illusory)
5. Individual spread Sharpes are ALL below 0.40 (no single spread carries sufficient signal)
6. HYG/LQD spread correlation to Family 1 credit strategies > 0.50 (too correlated with existing portfolio)
7. March 2020 max drawdown > 15% (liquidity crisis produces correlated spread blowups)
8. DSR < 0.95

## Robustness Notes

The academic liquidity premium evidence has important limitations when
applied to ETFs:

- **ETF vs stock-level evidence**: Pastor-Stambaugh (2003) and Amihud
  (2002) document the liquidity premium at the individual stock level.
  ETF baskets aggregate hundreds of stocks, diversifying away much of
  the idiosyncratic liquidity risk that drives the premium. The ETF-level
  liquidity spread may capture only 20-40% of the academic estimate.
- **Short-selling costs**: Shorting SPY, EFA, and LQD has near-zero borrow
  cost. However, shorting costs for ETFs can increase during stress
  periods (exactly when the strategy exits), introducing asymmetric
  transaction costs.
- **Correlation in crisis**: All three spreads (IWM/SPY, EEM/EFA, HYG/LQD)
  widen simultaneously during liquidity crises. The liquidity index should
  signal contraction before the worst of the spread widening, but if the
  crisis is sudden (March 2020), all three spreads can gap against the
  position before the weekly rebalance fires.
- **Mitigation**: Add a VIX > 35 emergency close — if VIX spikes above
  35 intraday, close all spreads immediately regardless of the weekly
  rebalance schedule.

## 2022 Stress Test

2022 was a liquidity contraction year: Fed tightening, QT, rising VIX,
widening credit spreads. Expected behavior:

- Liquidity index should signal contraction by Q1 2022 (VIX rising, Amihud
  rising, credit spreads widening, volume declining).
- Strategy should be FLAT for most of 2022 (no spread positions during
  contraction).
- Key risk: brief liquidity expansion signals in summer 2022 (the "bear
  market rally" of Jul-Aug) could trigger spread entry before the Sep-Oct
  resumption of contraction. The 0.5 SD threshold should filter these
  shallow expansions, but this is the critical test.
- All three spreads widened in 2022: IWM underperformed SPY by ~5%, EEM
  underperformed EFA by ~8%, HYG underperformed LQD by ~4%. Being flat
  during this period would have been the correct positioning.
