## 1. Critical Issue: 8 Trades

This is the central problem. With only 8 trades over 50 months, every downstream
robustness test is statistically meaningless. The Phase 1 Kill Chain (alpha-hunting-framework.md)
requires >25 trades in 5 years at the "not-obviously-broken" gate. This strategy fails
that check with 8 trades — it should not have advanced to full robustness.

### Why 8 trades is fatal

- **Sharpe estimation error**: For N trades, the standard error of the Sharpe ratio
  is approximately `SE(SR) = sqrt((1 + SR^2/2) / N)`. With N=8, SR=1.08:
  `SE = sqrt((1 + 0.58) / 8) = sqrt(0.198) = 0.44`. The 95% CI for the true Sharpe
  is approximately `1.08 +/- 0.87`, i.e., **[0.21, 1.95]**. This interval spans from
  barely positive to exceptional — the point estimate is nearly meaningless.

- **Win rate**: 3 wins out of 8 trades. A binomial test: `P(3 or fewer wins | p=0.5) = 0.363`.
  The 37.5% win rate is not significantly different from a coin flip (p=0.36).
  A true positive-expectancy strategy could easily show 3/8 wins by chance.

- **Survivorship in returns**: The strategy made 9.73% total with 2.5% time-in-market.
  This is ~2.24% annualized. SPY buy-and-hold returned ~40% over the same period.
  The Sharpe looks good only because the strategy is in cash 97.5% of the time,
  which eliminates volatility from the denominator during most of the backtest.

## 2. CPCV Assessment: CANNOT RUN

CPCV with N=6 groups, k=2 test groups requires at least ~4 trades per group
(24 total) to have meaningful train/test splits. With 8 trades total:

- Most CPCV folds would have 0-2 trades in the test set
- A fold with 0 test trades produces undefined Sharpe
- A fold with 1-2 test trades produces a "Sharpe" that is pure noise
- The 15 combinations would produce a distribution dominated by undefined/extreme values

**Verdict:** CPCV is **structurally inapplicable** to this strategy at current trade count.

Even the minimal CPCV configuration (N=4, k=1 = 4 folds) would average 2 trades
per fold — insufficient for any distributional inference.

## 3. DSR Assessment: MISLEADING

The reported DSR of 0.996 is technically correct in the formula but practically
meaningless. Here is why:

- **DSR with N=1 trial**: Only 1 experiment was run (trial_number=1). With N=1,
  the DSR formula's multiple-testing correction is essentially zero — there is no
  penalty for testing multiple strategies. DSR=0.996 with 1 trial just says
  "the observed Sharpe is positive with high probability given the data" — but the
  data has only 8 meaningful observations.

- **Low-N return distribution**: The DSR formula uses skewness and kurtosis of the
  *daily* return series, but 97.5% of daily returns are 0.0 (cash days). The
  non-zero returns are a sparse embedded series. The overall distribution is
  dominated by the mass at zero, making skewness/kurtosis estimates artifacts
  of the cash-heavy structure rather than properties of the signal.

- **DSR reliability bound**: Bailey & Lopez de Prado (2014) note that SR estimates
  require T >> 1 where T is the number of independent return observations. With
  only ~25 non-zero return days out of 1,050+ total days, effective T ~ 25.
  This is marginal even for basic Sharpe estimation.

**Verdict:** DSR PASS is **unreliable**. The 0.996 value reflects the formula's
behavior with minimal trials and a cash-dominated return series, not genuine
statistical confidence in the signal.

## 4. PBO Assessment: CANNOT RUN

PBO via CSCV requires partitioning the data into 16 sub-periods and evaluating
multiple strategy variants across all C(16,8) = 12,870 combinations. This requires:

1. Multiple strategy variants (parameter grid) to compare
2. Enough trades in each sub-period for the variant ranking to be meaningful

With 8 trades over 50 months, most 16-period sub-periods (~3 months each) would
contain 0-1 trades. Variant ranking would be noise.

**Verdict:** PBO is **structurally inapplicable**.

## 5. Perturbation Assessment: HIGH FRAGILITY RISK

The strategy has 3 key parameters:

| Parameter | Value | Perturbation Range | Risk |
|-----------|-------|--------------------|------|
| spike_threshold | 0.20 (20%) | 0.16 - 0.24 | HIGH |
| spike_exit_vix | 20.0 | 16.0 - 24.0 | HIGH |
| target_weight | 0.90 | 0.72 - 1.00 | LOW |

**spike_threshold perturbation** is the critical concern:
- At 0.20 (20%): 8 trades in 50 months
- At 0.16 (16%): Likely 12-20 trades (lower bar catches more spikes)
- At 0.24 (24%): Likely 3-5 trades (higher bar misses most spikes)
- At 0.12 (12%): Likely 30+ trades but captures many non-spike moves

The trade count is extremely sensitive to the spike threshold. A +-20% perturbation
(0.16 to 0.24) could change trade count from ~15 to ~4. When the number of trades
varies by 3-4x across the perturbation range, the Sharpe ratio comparison is
meaningless — you are comparing different sample sizes, not different strategies.

**spike_exit_vix perturbation**:
- At 20.0: exits when VIX normalizes
- At 16.0: holds much longer (VIX rarely drops below 16) — could hold through drawdowns
- At 24.0: exits almost immediately after entry — very short holding period

This parameter dramatically changes holding period duration, which affects both
return magnitude and drawdown exposure.

**Verdict:** Without running the perturbation (no backtest engine available in this
analysis), the parameter sensitivity structure strongly suggests FRAGILITY. The
strategy's performance is likely highly sensitive to exact threshold values.

## 6. Cost Sensitivity: LIKELY SURVIVES (trivially)

The strategy trades SPY, which has ~1bps spread and deep liquidity. With only
8 round-trip trades over 50 months, transaction costs are negligible:
- At 5bps one-way: 8 trades x 2 legs x 5bps = 80bps total drag = 0.08% over 50 months
- At 50bps one-way: 8 x 2 x 50bps = 800bps = 0.80% over 50 months

Even at 10x realistic costs, the strategy survives — but this is trivially true
because it barely trades. Cost sensitivity is not a meaningful test for strategies
with <10 trades.

**Verdict:** PASS, but vacuously — the test has no discriminating power.

## 8. Shuffled Returns Test: EXPECTED TO FAIL

The shuffled returns test randomizes signal dates while keeping the return series
intact. With a 20% VIX spike threshold, the strategy enters on ~8 specific days
out of 1,050. If we randomly select 8 entry days and hold for similar durations,
the expected return depends on:

- SPY's overall positive drift (~10-15% annualized) means randomly timed entries
  would also show positive returns on average
- The strategy's 37.5% win rate is below even random timing (which would be ~53-55%
  for multi-day SPY holds in a rising market)

The strategy's Sharpe advantage over random timing likely comes from the specific
*durations* (holding until VIX < 20) rather than the *entry timing*. This is
concerning — it suggests the exit rule, not the entry signal, drives returns.

**Verdict:** LIKELY FAIL — entry signal may not have predictive power above random.

## 10. Reformulation Recommendations

The VIX spike mean-reversion mechanism has genuine academic support (Whaley 2000,
Bollen & Whaley 2004). The problem is not the hypothesis — it is the signal
frequency. Three paths forward:

### Path A: Lower the threshold to generate more trades (RECOMMENDED)

- Reduce `spike_threshold` from 20% to 10-12%
- Expected trade count: 25-50 over 5 years
- Risk: More false positives (mild VIX pops that don't represent genuine fear)
- Mitigation: Add confirming filters (RSI oversold, VIX percentile rank)
- This is essentially what H7.1 (`vix-percentile-spike-reversion`) attempts —
  it adds RSI(5) < 30 and VIX 90th percentile as confirming filters

### Path B: Expand the asset universe

- Apply the same signal (VIX spike > X%) to QQQ, IWM, EFA in addition to SPY
- Each asset gets the same entry signal (VIX spike) but with asset-specific returns
- Pooled trade count across 4 assets: ~32 trades at 20% threshold
- Risk: Trades across assets are highly correlated (same entry signal)
- Mitigation: Use as a single "composite VIX spike" strategy, not 4 strategies

### Path C: Combine all F4 signals into a unified vol regime strategy

- Merge H4.1 (VIX term structure regime), H4.3 (VIX spike), and the VIX
  percentile approach (H7.1) into one multi-signal vol regime strategy
- The unified strategy enters on ANY qualified vol signal, exits on consolidated
  normalization criteria
- Expected trade count: 30-60 over 5 years
- Risk: Different signals may have conflicting directional implications
- Mitigation: Require signal agreement (e.g., backwardation + spike + oversold)

### Path D: Extend backtest to 10+ years

- VIX data is available since 1990. A 10-year backtest (2016-2026) would capture
  the COVID crash (2020), volmageddon (2018), and the 2022 bear market
- Expected trade count at 20% threshold: ~15-25 over 10 years
- Risk: Still below 25-trade minimum; older data may reflect different market structure
- Combined with Path A (12% threshold + 10yr window): ~50-100 trades

### Recommended next step

**Pursue Path A first** — reformulate as `vix-spike-mean-reversion-v2` with
`spike_threshold: 0.12`, confirming filters from H7.1 (RSI oversold + VIX percentile),
and a 7-10 year backtest window. This should yield 40-80 trades, which is sufficient
for CPCV and perturbation testing. If it passes robustness, it replaces both H4.3
and H7.1 as the canonical F4 VIX spike strategy.
