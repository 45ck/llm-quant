## System Context

### The llm-quant System

llm-quant is a systematic macro trading system managing a $100,000 paper trading portfolio across 39 tradeable assets spanning six asset classes:

- **US Equity ETFs (17)**: SPY, QQQ, IWM, DIA, VTI, IVV, XLK, XLF, XLE, XLV, XLI, XLY, XLP, XLU, XLRE, XLB, XLC
- **International Equity (3)**: VGK, EEM, EFA
- **Fixed Income (6)**: TLT, IEF, SHY, LQD, HYG, TIP
- **Commodities (3)**: GLD, SLV, USO
- **Crypto (5)**: BTC-USD, ETH-USD, SOL-USD, XRP-USD, ADA-USD
- **Forex (5)**: EURUSD=X, GBPUSD=X, USDJPY=X, AUDUSD=X, USDCHF=X

The system runs Python 3.12 with Polars DataFrames for computation and DuckDB for persistent storage. Technical indicators are computed in `src/llm_quant/data/indicators.py` and currently include SMA(20/50/200), Bollinger Bands(20,2), RSI(14), MACD(12,26,9), and ATR(14). Market data is stored in the `market_data_daily` table with columns `(symbol, date, open, high, low, close, volume, adj_close, sma_20, sma_50, rsi_14, macd, macd_signal, macd_hist, atr_14)`.

The decision pipeline flows: `scripts/build_context.py` (fetches data, computes indicators, assembles JSON context) -> Claude LLM (regime assessment, signal generation) -> `scripts/execute_decision.py` (risk checks, execution, persistence). Regime classification uses VIX level + TLT/SHY yield spread to categorize markets as `risk_on`, `risk_off`, or `transition` (implemented in `src/llm_quant/brain/context.py`).

Performance targets: Sharpe > 0.8, max drawdown < 15%, Sortino > 1.0, Calmar > 0.5.

### Current Risk Architecture

Eight pre-trade checks in `src/llm_quant/risk/limits.py`, orchestrated by `RiskManager.check_trade()` in `src/llm_quant/risk/manager.py`: (1) position size max 2% NAV, (2) position weight max 10%/5%/8% by asset class, (3) gross exposure max 200%, (4) net exposure max 100%, (5) sector concentration max 30%, (6) cash reserve min 5%, (7) stop-loss required, (8) drawdown circuit breaker at 15% with 3% buffer. Each returns `RiskCheckResult(passed, rule, message, current_value, limit_value)`. Config in `config/risk.toml` under `[limits]`.

### The Critical Gap This Research Addresses

**There is no tail risk measure. The 15% max drawdown limit is aspirational and purely backward-looking. It triggers only after the drawdown has already occurred.**

The current `check_drawdown_limit()` blocks new BUY signals when drawdown exceeds `max_drawdown_pct - 3%` (i.e., at -12% drawdown for a 15% limit). This is reactive, not predictive:

- By the time the 12% drawdown triggers the block, the portfolio has already lost $12,000 of its $100,000.
- The check does not assess whether the current portfolio *could* lose 15% in the next week given its composition and current market conditions. It only checks whether it *has already lost* 12%.
- There is no mechanism to scale down risk *before* a drawdown materializes. A portfolio concentrated in high-beta equity ETFs during a VIX spike has far higher expected tail loss than a bond-heavy portfolio, but both pass the drawdown check identically if they have not yet drawn down.

What is needed is a **forward-looking tail risk measure** that estimates the potential loss at a given confidence level, given the current portfolio composition and recent market behavior. Conditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR, also called Expected Shortfall) is the natural choice because:

1. It is a **coherent risk measure** (satisfies subadditivity, unlike VaR)
2. It answers the question: "Given that we are in the worst X% of scenarios, what is the expected loss?"
3. It can be computed from historical simulation using the existing `market_data_daily` table -- no parametric distributional assumptions required
4. It can serve as a **binding pre-trade constraint** that scales down proposed BUY sizes when the portfolio's tail risk is elevated

## Research Question

Can a fast CVaR (Expected Shortfall) computation using historical simulation serve as a binding pre-trade constraint that limits left-tail exposure, and does this reduce the frequency and severity of multi-day drawdown sequences?

Specifically:
1. What is the empirical CVaR(95%) of the portfolio under various compositions, and how does it compare to the 15% drawdown limit?
2. Does enforcing a CVaR limit (e.g., 1-day 95% ES < 3% NAV) as a pre-trade constraint reduce multi-day drawdown frequency by 40-60%?
3. What is the optimal CVaR limit that balances drawdown protection against trade throughput (false-positive blocking rate)?
4. How does stress-scenario-conditional CVaR (computing CVaR using only crisis-period returns) compare to unconditional CVaR as a risk measure?

## Hypothesis

A forward-looking CVaR limit, computed from historical simulation at the 95% confidence level, with a threshold of 3% NAV for 1-day Expected Shortfall, enforced as a pre-trade constraint, will:

- **Reduce the frequency of multi-day drawdown sequences** (defined as 3+ consecutive days of portfolio loss exceeding 0.5% NAV per day) by 40-60%
- **Reduce the average depth of drawdown events** by 25-35%
- **Reduce max drawdown** toward the 10-12% range (from the current uncontrolled ~15%+ risk)
- **Preserve Sharpe ratio within 10%** of unconstrained levels, because the CVaR constraint primarily blocks trades that would increase tail risk, not trades with high expected return
- **Operate as a soft constraint** (proportional scaling, not hard block): When 1-day CVaR(95%) exceeds 3% NAV, all proposed BUY sizes are scaled down proportionally by `min(1.0, cvar_limit / current_cvar)`, rather than rejected outright. This reduces the false-positive problem.

The enforcement mechanism:
```python
if portfolio_cvar_95 > cvar_limit:
    scale_factor = cvar_limit / portfolio_cvar_95  # < 1.0
    for signal in buy_signals:
        signal.target_weight *= scale_factor
```

Additionally, named stress scenarios (COVID, 2022 rates, crypto winter, SVB) will be computed as conditional CVaR estimates and stored for regime-conditional risk assessment. When the current regime matches a historical stress scenario, the stress-conditional CVaR is used instead of the unconditional estimate.

## Methodology

### Phase 1: CVaR Computation Engine (Week 1-2)

1. **Fetch 5 years of daily returns** for all 39 assets from the `market_data_daily` DuckDB table. Compute log returns: `r_i,t = ln(close_i,t / close_i,t-1)`.

2. **Implement `compute_portfolio_cvar()`** using historical simulation. The algorithm: extract the most recent `window` days of log returns for each symbol, pivot to wide format, compute portfolio returns as `r_portfolio,t = sum(w_i * r_i,t)`, sort ascending, VaR(95%) = 5th percentile, CVaR(95%) = mean of all returns below VaR(95%), convert to positive loss.

3. **Validate computation** against known benchmarks:
   - For a 100% SPY portfolio, VaR(95%) should be approximately 1.5-2.0% over 1 day (based on SPY's annualized vol of ~15-18%)
   - For a 60/40 SPY/TLT portfolio, VaR(95%) should be approximately 0.8-1.2% over 1 day
   - Compare against parametric VaR (assuming normal distribution) to quantify the fat-tail contribution captured by historical simulation

4. **Performance benchmark**: The CVaR computation must complete in under 100ms for a portfolio of 15 positions using a 252-day window. Profile using Polars vectorized operations. If too slow, pre-compute and cache the portfolio return series.

### Phase 2: Stress Scenario Construction (Week 2-3)

5. **Define named stress scenarios** with exact date ranges:

   | Scenario | Start Date | End Date | Duration (days) | Description |
   |----------|-----------|----------|-----------------|-------------|
   | COVID Crash | 2020-02-19 | 2020-03-23 | 23 | Global pandemic sell-off. SPY -34%, VIX to 82. |
   | 2022 Rates Shock | 2022-01-03 | 2022-06-16 | 116 | Fed tightening cycle. SPY -24%, TLT -22%, crypto -70%. |
   | Crypto Winter | 2021-11-10 | 2022-11-21 | 262 | BTC -77%, ETH -80%. Equity impact limited. |
   | SVB Crisis | 2023-03-06 | 2023-03-13 | 5 | Banking stress. XLF -15%, VIX spike to 30. |

6. **Compute stress-conditional CVaR**: For each stress scenario, extract daily returns during that period and compute CVaR using only those returns. This answers: "What would the current portfolio lose if we re-entered that scenario?"

7. **Store stress scenario definitions and results** in two new DuckDB tables: `stress_scenarios` (scenario_name PK, start_date, end_date, description, spy_return, vix_peak) and `stress_cvar_results` (computation_date + scenario_name PK, var_95, cvar_95, var_99, cvar_99, worst_day).

8. **Compute max-loss statistics per scenario**: For each stress scenario, compute the maximum 1-day, 5-day, and 21-day loss for the current portfolio composition if it had been held through the scenario unchanged. These become the headline risk numbers for the portfolio manager.

### Phase 3: CVaR Constraint Design (Week 3-4)

9. **Design the pre-trade CVaR check** with two modes:

   **Mode 1 -- Hard Block**: `check_portfolio_cvar(current_cvar_95, cvar_limit) -> RiskCheckResult`. Standard pass/fail like existing checks. Blocks all BUY signals when CVaR exceeds limit.

   **Mode 2 -- Proportional Scaling** (preferred): `scale_signals_by_cvar(signals, current_cvar_95, cvar_limit) -> list[TradeSignal]`. When CVaR is at 4% and limit is 3%, scale_factor = 3/4 = 0.75. All BUY target_weights are multiplied by 0.75; SELL/CLOSE pass through unchanged. This is a new pattern for the risk system -- soft rejection rather than hard block.

10. **Calibrate the CVaR limit**: Grid search over CVaR limits from 1.0% to 5.0% NAV in steps of 0.25%:
    - For each limit, replay historical portfolio decisions with the CVaR constraint active
    - Compute: number of scaling events, average scale factor, resulting drawdown profile, resulting Sharpe
    - Plot the Pareto frontier of Sharpe vs. max drawdown as CVaR limit varies

11. **Evaluate both modes**: Compare hard-block (Mode 1) vs. proportional scaling (Mode 2) on the same historical data. The hypothesis is that proportional scaling produces better Sharpe because it reduces trade size rather than eliminating trades entirely. Verify this.

### Phase 4: Regime-Conditional CVaR (Week 4-5)

12. **Compute regime-conditional CVaR estimates**: Instead of using the full 252-day window, use regime-specific windows:
    - **risk_on regime**: Use only risk_on days from the lookback (optimistic CVaR)
    - **risk_off regime**: Use only risk_off days from the lookback (pessimistic CVaR)
    - **transition regime**: Use the full window (unconditional CVaR)
    - **stress override**: When VIX > 30, use the worst stress scenario's CVaR instead of historical simulation

    The idea is that during risk_off, the CVaR computed from calm-period returns understates the true risk. Using stress-conditional returns provides a more conservative estimate when it matters most.

13. **Compare CVaR approaches**:

    | Approach | Description | Expected Properties |
    |----------|-------------|---------------------|
    | Unconditional | Full 252-day window | Stable, slow to react to regime change |
    | Regime-conditional | Risk_on/off/transition windows | More responsive, potentially noisy |
    | Stress-scenario max | Max CVaR across all named stress scenarios | Very conservative, may over-restrict |
    | Weighted blend | 0.5 * unconditional + 0.5 * regime-conditional | Balanced |

    Evaluate each approach by its ability to predict subsequent 5-day drawdowns (measured by rank correlation between CVaR estimate and realized subsequent loss).

## Literature Review

### Core Papers

1. **Rockafellar, R.T. & Uryasev, S. (2000).** "Optimization of Conditional Value-at-Risk." *Journal of Risk*, 2(3), 21-41.
   - **Extract**: Foundational paper establishing CVaR optimization. They show that CVaR can be minimized using linear programming when the loss distribution is represented by scenarios (historical simulation). The key result: minimizing CVaR simultaneously minimizes VaR because CVaR >= VaR. They provide the auxiliary variable formulation that makes CVaR optimization tractable.
   - **Application**: Their scenario-based approach is directly applicable to our historical simulation engine. Use their formulation to verify that our CVaR computation is correct: CVaR(alpha) = VaR(alpha) + E[L - VaR(alpha) | L > VaR(alpha)]. Their linear programming formulation could be used in a future extension to optimize portfolio weights subject to CVaR constraints.

2. **Rockafellar, R.T. & Uryasev, S. (2002).** "Conditional Value-at-Risk for General Loss Distributions." *Journal of Banking and Finance*, 26(7), 1443-1471.
   - **Extract**: Extends the 2000 paper to general (non-discrete) loss distributions. Proves that CVaR is a coherent risk measure (satisfying monotonicity, subadditivity, positive homogeneity, translation invariance). The subadditivity property is critical: the CVaR of the portfolio is less than or equal to the sum of individual CVaRs, meaning diversification is always rewarded.
   - **Application**: The coherence property justifies using CVaR as a portfolio-level constraint. Unlike VaR, which can penalize diversification (VaR of a portfolio can exceed the sum of individual VaRs in rare cases), CVaR always rewards it. This means our constraint correctly incentivizes diversification.

3. **Agarwal, V. & Naik, N.Y. (2004).** "Risks and Portfolio Decisions Involving Hedge Funds." *Review of Financial Studies*, 17(1), 63-98.
   - **Extract**: Demonstrates that mean-variance analysis is inadequate for portfolios with significant left-tail risk (as in multi-asset portfolios including crypto and commodities). CVaR-based optimization produces meaningfully different allocations than mean-variance, with lower realized tail losses. Empirical evidence from hedge fund portfolio construction.
   - **Application**: Our 39-asset universe includes crypto (extreme left tails), commodities (fat tails), and credit (HYG, LQD -- jump-to-default risk). All of these violate the normality assumption underlying simpler risk measures. Agarwal & Naik's evidence supports using CVaR over variance-based measures for our portfolio.

4. **McNeil, A.J., Frey, R., & Embrechts, P. (2015).** *Quantitative Risk Management: Concepts, Techniques and Tools.* Princeton University Press, Chapters 2 & 5.
   - **Extract**: Chapter 2 covers risk measures (VaR, ES/CVaR, spectral risk measures) with mathematical rigor. Chapter 5 covers extreme value theory (EVT) for modeling tails beyond what historical simulation can capture. The semi-parametric approach (historical simulation in the body, EVT for the extreme tail) provides better estimates of very high quantiles (99.9%) where historical data is sparse.
   - **Application**: For our 95% CVaR using 252 days of data, we have approximately 12-13 observations in the tail. This is borderline sufficient. If we move to 99% CVaR, we have only 2-3 observations -- insufficient without EVT. Use their guidance to determine whether 95% with historical simulation is adequate for our use case, or whether a semi-parametric approach is needed.

### Supplementary Papers

5. **Acerbi, C. & Tasche, D. (2002).** "On the Coherence of Expected Shortfall." *Journal of Banking and Finance*, 26(7), 1487-1503.
   - **Extract**: Proves that Expected Shortfall (= CVaR for continuous distributions) is the minimal coherent risk measure dominating VaR. Discusses the estimation properties of ES and shows that historical simulation estimates converge at rate 1/sqrt(N).
   - **Application**: Use their convergence rate result to determine minimum sample size. For a 5% tail with 252 observations, the effective sample size in the tail is ~12. Standard error of the ES estimate is approximately sigma_tail / sqrt(12), which is large. This quantifies the estimation uncertainty and motivates using larger lookback windows or stress scenarios to supplement.

6. **Alexander, C. & Baptista, A. (2004).** "A Comparison of VaR and CVaR Constraints on Portfolio Selection with the Mean-Variance Model." *Journal of Economic Dynamics and Control*, 28(7), 1159-1193.
   - **Extract**: Shows that CVaR-constrained portfolios differ from VaR-constrained portfolios primarily in their treatment of concentrated positions. CVaR constraints discourage concentration more effectively because they account for the severity of losses beyond the VaR threshold, not just their probability.
   - **Application**: Validates our choice of CVaR over VaR. Our portfolio includes highly concentrated risk factors (equity ETFs that are correlated, crypto that is correlated). CVaR will penalize this concentration more than VaR would.

## Evaluation Criteria

### Success Thresholds

| Criterion | Threshold | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Multi-day drawdown frequency reduction | >= 30% | Primary objective. Must meaningfully reduce the number of extended loss sequences. |
| Max drawdown improvement | >= 15% relative reduction | CVaR constraint should push max DD from ~15% toward 10-12%. |
| Sharpe ratio preservation | <= -10% relative to baseline | The constraint must not excessively impair returns. Proportional scaling should help. |
| CVaR prediction accuracy | Rank correlation >= 0.30 with subsequent 5-day loss | CVaR must have some predictive power for near-term tail risk. Below 0.30 suggests noise. |
| Computation time | < 200ms per portfolio evaluation | Must not materially slow the decision pipeline. Historical simulation with Polars should be fast. |
| Stress scenario coverage | CVaR signals elevated risk in >= 3 of 4 named scenarios | The measure must detect known stress events. Failure on > 1 scenario indicates insufficient sensitivity. |

### Failure Conditions

- If CVaR prediction accuracy (rank correlation with subsequent 5-day loss) is below 0.15, the measure has essentially no predictive power and the constraint is random noise.
- If proportional scaling reduces Sharpe by more than 15% without proportional drawdown reduction, the cost-benefit is unfavorable.
- If CVaR is persistently elevated (> 60% of trading days trigger scaling), the limit is too tight and the constraint is acting as a permanent drag rather than a tail-risk filter.
- If CVaR fails to signal elevated risk during 2 or more of the 4 named stress scenarios, the historical simulation approach is insufficient and a parametric or stress-testing approach is needed.

## Anti-Overfitting Warnings

### Specific Pitfalls for This Research

1. **CVaR limit calibrated to known crises**: If the 3% limit is chosen because it "would have prevented" the COVID drawdown, that is textbook overfitting. **Mitigation**: Walk-forward optimization -- calibrate on 2019-2021, validate on 2022-2023, verify on 2024-2025.

2. **Historical simulation assumes the past represents the future**: The worst 5% of historical returns may not capture future tail events. **Mitigation**: Supplement with stress-scenario conditional CVaR. Acknowledge that the CVaR constraint is necessary but not sufficient.

3. **Confidence level selection**: At 95% with 252 observations, ~12 observations define the tail. At 99%, only 2-3 -- far too noisy. **Mitigation**: Use 95% as primary; report 99% as supplementary. Compute bootstrap confidence intervals for the estimate.

4. **Lookback window cliff effects**: A stress event dropping out of the 252-day window causes a sudden CVaR decrease. **Mitigation**: Test exponentially weighted windows (recent data weighted higher, old data never fully drops) and longer 504-day windows.

5. **Proportional scaling path dependency**: Scaling down today's trades lowers tomorrow's CVaR, allowing larger trades, creating oscillation. **Mitigation**: Constrain the post-trade CVaR (what it would be after proposed trades execute), not just the pre-trade value.

6. **CVaR treats all tail events equally**: A 3% loss from mean-reverting rotation differs from a 3% loss in systemic crisis. **Mitigation**: Use regime-conditional CVaR and augment with serial correlation adjustment for multi-day risk.

7. **Double-constraint trap**: CVaR + drawdown circuit breaker together may create a state where the portfolio cannot add risk even when conditions improve. **Mitigation**: Design a hysteresis band (trigger at 3.0% CVaR, release at 2.5%) to prevent on/off switching.

8. **Computational cost**: The 252x39 matrix operation is trivial for Polars today, but marginal CVaR per signal would multiply the cost. **Mitigation**: Pre-compute the return matrix once per session in `build_context.py`. Set 200ms budget for all CVaR computations per session.
