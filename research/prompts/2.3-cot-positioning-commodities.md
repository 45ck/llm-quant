**Current indicators** (computed in `src/llm_quant/data/indicators.py`): SMA(20/50/200), Bollinger Bands (20-period, 2 std), RSI(14) via Wilder's smoothing, MACD(12,26,9), ATR(14). All computed per-symbol via `group_by`/`over` partitioning in pure Polars expressions. These are purely price-based indicators with no positioning data.

**Current regime classification** (in `src/llm_quant/brain/context.py`): Uses VIX level (via `_get_vix()`), TLT/SHY yield spread proxy (via `_get_yield_spread()`), and SPY SMA50/SMA200 trend (via `_get_spy_trend()`). **Pipeline**: `scripts/build_context.py` fetches data, computes indicators, builds `MarketContext`, renders prompts. Claude LLM produces `TradingDecision` with `MarketRegime` and up to 5 `TradeSignal` objects. `scripts/execute_decision.py` runs risk checks and executes approved trades.

**Directly affected assets**: GLD (Gold), SLV (Silver), USO (Crude Oil), EURUSD=X, GBPUSD=X, USDJPY=X, AUDUSD=X, USDCHF=X. These 8 assets represent commodity and forex exposure in the portfolio and are the primary targets for COT-based signals.

**Targets**: Sharpe > 0.8, max drawdown < 15%, Sortino > 1.0, Calmar > 0.5. Primary benchmark: 60/40 SPY/TLT.

**Why P1 priority**: COT data is the highest-quality free positioning dataset available. Unlike sentiment (noisy, requires NLP) or macro indicators (lagging), COT provides actual reported positions from regulated futures markets. The data has been published weekly since 1986, providing a deep history for backtesting. The CFTC mandates reporting -- this is not self-reported survey data.

## Research Question

Do changes in net speculative positioning reported in the CFTC Commitments of Traders (COT) report provide a leading signal for directional moves in GLD, SLV, USO, and forex pairs over 4-8 week horizons? Specifically:

1. Does the COT Index (net speculative positioning as a percentile of its own 3-year history) identify crowded positioning extremes that precede reversals?
2. Is the contrarian signal (fade crowded speculators) stronger than the momentum signal (follow smart money), and under which regime conditions?
3. Does combining COT positioning with the existing price-based indicators (SMA, RSI, MACD) produce materially better risk-adjusted returns than either signal set alone?
4. What is the optimal signal horizon -- does COT positioning lead price by 2 weeks, 4 weeks, or 8 weeks?

---

## Hypothesis

When non-commercial (speculative) traders reach multi-year extreme net positioning in commodity and forex futures, positions are crowded and prone to rapid unwinding. The COT report reveals this crowding with a weekly lag (data as of Tuesday, released Friday), but the unwinding process takes 4-8 weeks, providing ample time for a weekly-rebalanced macro strategy to exploit.

**Falsifiable predictions**:

1. **Contrarian signal**: COT Index < 20 (speculators historically short, crowded to the downside) predicts positive forward 4-week returns with a hit rate > 55%. COT Index > 80 (speculators historically long, crowded to the upside) predicts negative forward 4-week returns with a hit rate > 55%.

2. **Signal lead time**: Peak Granger-causality from COT Index changes to weekly returns occurs at the 2-4 week lag, confirming that positioning leads price.

3. **Combined superiority**: A signal that combines COT Index extremes with price-based confirmation (e.g., COT Index < 20 AND RSI < 30 for a bullish entry) achieves Sharpe improvement > 0.15 over a price-only baseline on the commodity/forex subset.

4. **Cross-asset consistency**: The contrarian COT signal works on at least 4 of the 5 tested contracts (Gold, Silver, Crude Oil, EUR/USD, USD/JPY). A signal that works on only 1-2 contracts is not a systematic signal.

5. **Regime interaction**: The COT contrarian signal is strongest during `transition` regimes (markets are uncertain, positioning extremes resolve via sharp reversals) and weakest during strong `risk_on` trends (speculators may be right to be long in a sustained bull market).

**Expected magnitude**: COT is a medium-frequency, medium-strength signal. Expected IC in the range of 0.05-0.12 at the 4-week horizon. If IC exceeds 0.20, suspect overfitting.

## Methodology

### Phase 1: COT Data Acquisition and Parsing (Week 1)

1. **Download COT data** from the CFTC website. Two report types are relevant:
   - **Legacy report** (Futures Only): Simpler, longer history (1986-present). Breaks positions into Commercial, Non-Commercial, and Non-Reportable.
   - **Disaggregated report** (since 2006): More granular. Breaks Non-Commercial into Managed Money, Other Reportable, and Non-Reportable. Preferred for post-2006 analysis.

2. **CFTC data URLs**:
   - Current year: https://www.cftc.gov/dea/newcot/deacot.zip (Legacy) and https://www.cftc.gov/dea/newcot/f_disagg.zip (Disaggregated)
   - Historical archive: https://www.cftc.gov/MarketReports/CommitmentsofTraders/HistoricalCompressed/index.htm
   - Files are fixed-width text or CSV, released every Friday at 3:30pm ET for positions as of the preceding Tuesday.

3. **Contract specifications** (CFTC commodity codes):

   | Asset (llm-quant) | Futures Contract | CFTC Code | Exchange |
   |-------------------|-----------------|-----------|----------|
   | GLD | Gold COMEX | 084691 | COMEX |
   | SLV | Silver COMEX | 084651 | COMEX |
   | USO | Crude Oil WTI | 067651 | NYMEX |
   | EURUSD=X | Euro FX | 099741 | CME |
   | USDJPY=X | Japanese Yen | 097741 | CME |
   | GBPUSD=X | British Pound | 096742 | CME |
   | AUDUSD=X | Australian Dollar | 232741 | CME |
   | USDCHF=X | Swiss Franc | 092741 | CME |

4. **Parser design**: Read CFTC CSV files. Extract per-contract per-week: `report_date` (Tuesday), `release_date` (Friday), `non_commercial_long/short/net` (speculative positions), `commercial_long/short/net` (hedger positions), and `open_interest`.

5. **Store in DuckDB**: Create table `cot_positioning` with columns: `report_date`, `release_date`, `cftc_code`, `symbol` (llm-quant symbol), `non_commercial_net`, `commercial_net`, `open_interest`, `non_commercial_long`, `non_commercial_short`, `commercial_long`, `commercial_short`. Primary key: `(report_date, cftc_code)`.

### Phase 2: COT Index Construction (Week 1-2)

1. **COT Index definition**: For each contract, compute:
   ```
   COT_Index(t) = (Net(t) - Min(Net, t-156w..t)) / (Max(Net, t-156w..t) - Min(Net, t-156w..t)) * 100
   ```
   Where `Net(t)` = non_commercial_net at week t, and the lookback window is 156 weeks (3 years). This normalizes positioning to a 0-100 scale where:
   - **COT Index < 20**: Speculators are unusually short (historically bearish). Contrarian interpretation: bullish.
   - **COT Index > 80**: Speculators are unusually long (historically bullish). Contrarian interpretation: bearish.
   - **COT Index 20-80**: No extreme. No positioning-based signal.

2. **Alternative COT metrics to test**:
   - **COT momentum**: Week-over-week change in non_commercial_net. Captures the *rate of change* of positioning, not just the level.
   - **Commercial vs. speculative divergence**: When commercials (hedgers, considered "smart money" in commodities) are positioned opposite to speculators, the signal may be stronger.
   - **Open interest context**: Extreme positioning with rising open interest (new positions being established) vs. falling open interest (positions being closed) may have different implications.
   - **Net-to-OI ratio**: non_commercial_net / open_interest. Normalizes for contract growth over time.

3. **Lookback sensitivity**: Test COT Index with 52-week (1Y), 104-week (2Y), 156-week (3Y), and 260-week (5Y) lookbacks. The 156-week (3Y) is the standard in the literature; ensure results are not sensitive to this choice.

4. **Forward-fill to daily**: COT is weekly (Tuesday date, Friday release). For integration with a daily trading system, forward-fill the Friday release to the following Thursday. On Friday, update with new data. This ensures no look-ahead bias.

### Phase 3: Signal Analysis (Week 2-3)

1. **Univariate return prediction**:
   - For each contract, compute the correlation between COT Index and forward returns at 1-week, 2-week, 4-week, and 8-week horizons.
   - Use the ETF/forex price data already in the llm-quant DuckDB (fetched by `scripts/build_context.py` via `fetch_ohlcv()`).
   - Report Spearman rank correlation and p-value.

2. **Extreme-conditional returns**:
   - Compute mean forward returns when COT Index < 20, 20-80, and > 80.
   - Test significance of difference between extreme buckets using a two-sample t-test.
   - Plot histograms of forward returns conditional on COT extremes.

3. **Granger causality**:
   - Test whether lagged COT Index changes Granger-cause weekly returns (and vice versa).
   - Use 1-4 week lags.
   - Require F-test p-value < 0.05 for the COT -> returns direction, and confirm that returns -> COT is weaker (positioning follows price with a lag, but positioning *also* leads price at extremes).

4. **Information Coefficient (IC) analysis**:
   - Weekly cross-sectional IC: Spearman correlation between COT Index (across all 8 contracts) and forward N-week return.
   - Report mean IC, IC standard deviation, IC_IR (mean / std), and t-statistic.
   - Compare IC at different horizons to identify optimal signal horizon.

5. **Regime-conditional analysis**:
   - Split sample by llm-quant regime (risk_on, risk_off, transition).
   - Compute conditional IC within each regime.
   - Hypothesis: strongest in transition regimes.

### Phase 4: Combined Signal Construction (Week 3-4)

1. **COT + Price signal matrix**: Test 5 pre-specified combinations: COT-only (Index < 20 / > 80), COT + RSI (< 20 AND RSI < 40 / > 80 AND RSI > 60), COT + MACD (< 20 AND MACD crossover / > 80 AND MACD crossunder), COT + SMA (< 20 AND close > SMA50 / > 80 AND close < SMA50), and COT + RSI + SMA (triple confirmation). These combinations are specified *before* seeing results. Do not test exhaustive combinations and report the best.

2. **Signal weighting**: Test equal (50/50), COT-dominant (70/30), and price-dominant (30/70) weightings. Expect COT-dominant at 4-8 week horizons and price-dominant at shorter horizons.

3. **Position sizing integration**: Map COT Index to target_weight: Index < 10 = max allowed weight, 10-20 = 60% of max, 20-80 = hold, 80-90 = reduce to 40%, > 90 = close. All positions require stop-loss per risk.toml (5% default, 3% forex).

### Phase 5: Backtest (Week 4-5)

1. **Backtest specification**:
   - Universe: GLD, SLV, USO, EURUSD=X, USDJPY=X (5 assets with deep COT history).
   - Extend to GBPUSD=X, AUDUSD=X, USDCHF=X if initial results are positive.
   - Rebalance: Weekly on Friday (after COT release).
   - In-sample: 2006-2019 (14 years, covers GFC, commodity boom/bust, strong dollar).
   - Out-of-sample: 2020-2024 (5 years, covers COVID, inflation spike, rate hiking cycle).
   - Transaction costs: 5bps per trade for commodity ETFs, 2bps for forex.
   - Slippage: 0.5 * ATR(14) / close as a percentage.

2. **Strategy variants**: (A) COT-only contrarian (enter at extremes, exit at neutral), (B) Price-only baseline (existing SMA + RSI + MACD), (C) COT + Price combined (extremes confirmed by price indicators), (D) COT + Price + Regime filter. Report Sharpe, Sortino, max drawdown, Calmar, win rate, profit factor, trades/year, and average hold period for each variant.

3. **Per-asset breakdown**: Report all metrics for each of the 5 (or 8) assets individually. Aggregate numbers may hide that the signal works on gold but not crude oil.

### Phase 6: Robustness Testing (Week 5-6)

1. **Walk-forward optimization**: 5-year training window, 1-year test window, rolled forward annually. Report year-by-year out-of-sample returns.
2. **Structural break test**: Compare 2006-2019 IC vs. 2020-2024 IC. COVID changed commodity market dynamics; verify signal persistence.
3. **Lookback sensitivity**: Rerun with COT Index lookbacks of 52w, 104w, 156w, and 260w. Results should be robust across >= 3 of 4 windows.
4. **Threshold sensitivity**: Test at 15/85, 20/80, and 25/75. Results should not collapse if thresholds shift by 5 points.
5. **Calendar effects**: Verify no seasonal artifacts in precious metals and forex by testing per-quarter performance.
6. **Dollar regime**: Test whether COT provides incremental information beyond DXY momentum via regression: `forward_return ~ COT_Index + DXY_momentum + COT_Index * DXY_momentum`.

## Literature Review

### Sanders, Irwin & Merrin (2009), "Smart Money? The Forecasting Ability of CFTC Large Traders in Agricultural Futures Markets"
- **Journal**: Journal of Agricultural and Resource Economics, 34(2), 276-296
- **Key finding**: Examined whether "smart money" (large speculators/commercials) can forecast agricultural futures prices. Found that non-commercial traders' net positions have modest forecasting power for returns at 1-4 week horizons, but the economic significance after transaction costs is limited. Commercials (hedgers) have better timing on average than non-commercials (speculators).
- **Extract for this research**: Two critical takeaways: (1) The signal exists but is modest -- expect IC in the 0.05-0.10 range, not 0.20+. (2) Commercials may be the "smarter" money in agricultural markets. For our purposes (metals, energy, forex), test both non-commercial and commercial positioning as signals. The contrarian interpretation (fade non-commercial extremes) and the "smart money" interpretation (follow commercial positioning) may give different results by asset class.
- **Implication**: Do not assume the same COT interpretation works across all commodities. Gold and forex may differ from agricultural markets.

### Gorton, Hayashi & Rouwenhorst (2013), "The Fundamentals of Commodity Futures Returns"
- **Journal**: Review of Finance, 17(1), 35-105
- **Key finding**: Demonstrated that the basis (futures price / spot price - 1) and inventory levels are the fundamental drivers of commodity futures returns. The basis reflects storage costs, convenience yield, and risk premia. COT positioning is a noisy proxy for the basis.
- **Extract for this research**: The basis is more fundamental than COT positioning. Ideally, we would use basis data directly, but for ETFs (GLD, SLV, USO), we are trading the spot-equivalent, not futures. COT positioning captures the *risk premium* component of the basis -- when speculators are heavily long, they are accepting a lower risk premium (crowded trade). This is the theoretical foundation for the contrarian signal.
- **Implication**: The COT signal is theoretically grounded in risk premium dynamics, not just "crowd behavior." This gives us more confidence in its persistence compared to purely behavioral signals. However, the signal may weaken when inventory/storage dynamics dominate (e.g., USO during oil gluts).

### Dewally, Ederington & Fernando (2013), "Determinants of Trader Profits in Commodity Futures Markets"
- **Journal**: Review of Financial Studies, 26(10), 2648-2683 (Note: commonly cited under Journal of Commodity Markets; verify the exact outlet)
- **Key finding**: Analyzed profitability of different trader categories in commodity futures. Found that managed money (hedge funds) and swap dealers have different performance profiles. Managed money tends to be trend-following (momentum), while commercials tend to be contrarian (hedging against price moves). In trending markets, managed money profits; in mean-reverting markets, commercials profit.
- **Extract for this research**: This directly informs our regime-conditional hypothesis. During strong trends (risk_on with commodity exposure), following managed money positioning may work better. During range-bound or transition regimes, fading managed money positioning (contrarian) works better. Our signal should be regime-aware: contrarian in transition, momentum in strong trends.
- **Implication**: Do not use a single interpretation (always contrarian) across all regimes. The COT signal should flip from contrarian to momentum depending on the market regime. Test both interpretations and their regime interactions.

### Wang (2003), "The Behavior and Performance of Major Types of Futures Traders"
- **Journal**: Journal of Futures Markets, 23(1), 1-31
- **Key finding**: One of the earliest comprehensive studies of COT data. Found that large speculators (non-commercial) are trend followers whose positions move with recent price trends. Large hedgers (commercial) are contrarian. Small traders (non-reportable) are noise. Net speculative positioning Granger-causes returns at the 1-2 week horizon, but the effect reverses at longer horizons (4-8 weeks), consistent with the contrarian interpretation.
- **Extract for this research**: The reversal at 4-8 weeks is exactly our target horizon. The finding that speculators trend-follow and their positions mean-revert at longer horizons provides the causal mechanism: speculators pile into a trend, creating crowding. When the trend stalls, they unwind simultaneously, causing a reversal. Our 4-8 week signal horizon aligns with Wang's findings.
- **Implication**: Use 4-week as the primary signal horizon, with 2-week and 8-week as robustness checks. Expect the 1-week IC to be positive (momentum) and the 4-8 week IC to be negative (contrarian). If we observe a positive 4-week IC, the contrarian hypothesis is wrong for that contract.

## Evaluation Criteria

### Go/No-Go Thresholds

| Criterion | Threshold | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Combined Sharpe improvement (vs. price-only) | >= 0.15 | Primary evaluation metric |
| IC at 4-week horizon (cross-sectional) | > 0.05 | Minimum for a useful signal |
| IC t-statistic | > 2.0 | Statistical significance |
| Win rate at COT extremes | > 55% | Above random for a contrarian signal |
| Cross-asset consistency | Signal works on >= 4 of 5 contracts | Systematic, not asset-specific |
| Out-of-sample IC retention | > 60% of in-sample | Not overfit |
| Max drawdown of combined strategy | No more than 2% worse than price-only | Risk preservation |
| Implementation effort | < 25 hours | Low complexity; data is free and well-structured |

### Automatic No-Go Conditions

- If the 4-week IC is positive (extreme speculative longs predict *positive* returns), the contrarian hypothesis is wrong for that contract. If this holds for 3+ contracts, reject the entire signal.
- If the COT Index has >0.80 correlation with RSI(14) on the same asset, it adds no incremental information beyond what the price-based system already captures.
- If the signal only works on gold (GLD) and nothing else, it is not a systematic signal -- it is a gold-specific anomaly. Reject.
- If walk-forward out-of-sample performance is negative (losing money) in any 3-year window, the signal is unstable.
- If the combined Sharpe improvement exceeds +0.40, suspect overfitting. Require additional sub-period analysis.

### Partial Go Conditions

- If the signal works on commodities (GLD, SLV, USO) but not forex, integrate for commodities only.
- If the contrarian interpretation works only in transition/risk_off regimes and the momentum interpretation works in risk_on, implement a regime-switching interpretation.
- If COT-only is weak but COT + price combined is strong, integrate as a confirmation filter (boost conviction when COT agrees with price signal) rather than a standalone signal.

## Anti-Overfitting Warnings

### Specific Pitfalls for This Research

1. **Look-ahead bias in release dates**: COT data is reported as of Tuesday but released on Friday at 3:30pm ET. Never use Tuesday's data in a strategy that trades before Friday's close. The `release_date` column must be used for alignment, not `report_date`. This is the most critical data integrity issue.

2. **ETF vs. futures basis risk**: We trade GLD (ETF), not gold futures. GLD tracks the spot gold price but can diverge from futures due to roll costs, ETF expenses (0.40%/year for GLD), and creation/redemption dynamics. The COT signal is from futures markets; its relevance to the ETF depends on basis stability. Monitor GLD-futures basis and flag periods where it exceeds 50bps.

3. **USO roll contamination**: USO (United States Oil Fund) was restructured in 2020 during the negative oil price event. Before April 2020, USO held front-month WTI futures. After, it spreads across multiple months. COT data for WTI front-month may not align with USO's holdings post-2020. Consider treating pre/post April 2020 as separate regimes for USO.

4. **Survivorship bias in contract selection**: We selected 8 contracts where COT data exists and llm-quant has matching ETF/forex. We did not select the 8 contracts where COT works best. This is good -- but document the selection process to prevent accusations of cherry-picking.

5. **Lookback window overlap**: With a 156-week (3-year) lookback for the COT Index, adjacent weeks share 155 of 156 data points. This means the COT Index changes slowly and adjacent readings are highly autocorrelated. Serial correlation in the signal means:
   - Standard t-tests overstate significance. Use Newey-West standard errors or block bootstrap.
   - Trade frequency will be low (extremes persist for weeks). Expect 2-4 extreme signals per asset per year.
   - Walk-forward windows must be long enough (>= 1 year) to contain independent signal events.

6. **Structural changes in speculator composition**: Pre-2008, "non-commercial" was dominated by macro hedge funds and CTAs. Post-2008, pension funds and ETFs became large speculative positions. The Disaggregated report (2006+) separates "Managed Money" from "Other Reportable," which is better. But even within Managed Money, the composition has changed. A model calibrated on 2006-2012 data may not work on 2018-2024 data.

7. **Position limit changes**: The CFTC has periodically changed position reporting thresholds and position limits. This affects what positions appear in the COT report. Most recently, position limits on commodity futures were revised in 2021. Verify that the raw data is consistent across these regime changes.

8. **Small sample at extremes**: If the COT Index is < 20 only 10% of the time per contract, that is ~20 signals per asset over 10 years. With 5 assets, you have ~100 extreme events. This is a small sample for statistical testing. Do not slice the data further (by month, regime, etc.) without acknowledging the reduced power.

9. **Confounding with price momentum**: Speculators trend-follow, so extreme net longs coincide with recent price increases. The contrarian signal (price will reverse after extreme positioning) is partly an RSI/mean-reversion signal in disguise. Test whether COT provides incremental IC *after controlling for RSI and price momentum*. Run: `forward_return ~ COT_Index + RSI + SMA_trend + COT_Index * RSI`.

10. **Over-reliance on gold**: Gold (GLD) has the deepest COT history and the most literature support. There is a risk that any aggregate "COT works" conclusion is driven entirely by gold. Report gold results separately and test whether the signal works *excluding* gold. If the signal is gold-only, it is still useful (gold is in our universe) but the implementation scope is smaller.
