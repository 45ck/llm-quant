## System Context

This research targets **llm-quant**, a systematic macro trading system managing a $100,000 paper trading portfolio across 39 instruments spanning six asset classes. The system executes momentum and mean-reversion signals generated by an LLM (Claude) after ingesting a structured market context.

### Portfolio Universe

| Asset Class | Instruments | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| US Equity ETFs (17) | Broad market, sectors | SPY, QQQ, IWM, DIA, XLK, XLF, XLE, XLV, XLI, XLY, XLP, XLU, XLRE, XLB, XLC, VTI, IVV |
| International Equity (3) | Developed, EM | VGK, EEM, EFA |
| Fixed Income (6) | Duration spectrum, credit | TLT, IEF, SHY, LQD, HYG, TIP |
| Commodities (3) | Precious metals, energy | GLD, SLV, USO |
| Crypto (5) | Layer 1, payments | BTC-USD, ETH-USD, SOL-USD, XRP-USD, ADA-USD |
| Forex (5) | Major pairs | EURUSD=X, GBPUSD=X, USDJPY=X, AUDUSD=X, USDCHF=X |

### Relevant Code Paths

- **Signal generation**: `src/llm_quant/brain/context.py` -- `build_market_context()` assembles the latest close, SMA(20/50), RSI(14), MACD(12,26,9), ATR(14), VIX, and yield spread into a `MarketContext` object. Signals are generated by the LLM based on this context.
- **Indicator computation**: `src/llm_quant/data/indicators.py` -- `compute_indicators()` operates on daily OHLCV bars fetched from Yahoo Finance via `src/llm_quant/data/fetcher.py`. All indicators use closing prices; no intraday data is consumed.
- **Execution pipeline**: `scripts/build_context.py` fetches data if stale, computes indicators, builds the JSON context. `scripts/execute_decision.py` reads the LLM's JSON decision from stdin, passes signals through `src/llm_quant/risk/manager.py` (7 pre-trade checks), then calls `src/llm_quant/trading/executor.py` to fill at the latest available close price.
- **Risk limits** (from `config/risk.toml`): max 4% NAV per trade, 12% per position (5% crypto, 8% forex), 200% gross exposure, 100% net exposure, 30% sector concentration, 5% cash reserve, stop-loss required, 15% drawdown circuit breaker.
- **Current execution assumption**: The system fills trades at the latest daily close price stored in DuckDB. There is no explicit modeling of execution timing, slippage, or market impact.

### Current Gap

The system implicitly assumes zero slippage by executing at the last stored close price. For a $100k book, absolute dollar impact is small per trade (max $4,000 notional per trade), but systematic bias compounds across hundreds of trades. The system has no mechanism to choose between MOC, TWAP, or next-open execution, nor does it adjust execution strategy by asset class or volatility regime.

## Research Question

For a systematic macro strategy executing momentum signals at end-of-day on a $100,000 book spanning ETFs, crypto, and forex, **what is the measurable cost difference between executing at the close (MOC), executing in the final 30 minutes via TWAP, and executing at next-day open** -- and does this cost vary materially by asset class or VIX regime?

Specifically:

1. What is the median and 95th-percentile slippage (in basis points) between the 4:00 PM close and the next-day open across each of the six asset classes?
2. How does closing auction volume share relate to market impact for ETFs with varying AUM/ADV profiles?
3. For 24-hour assets (crypto, forex), is "close" even a meaningful concept, and what reference price minimizes tracking error against signal generation time?
4. Does VIX regime (below 15 = low vol, 15-25 = normal, above 25 = stress) shift the optimal execution window?

---

## Hypothesis

**Primary hypothesis**: MOC orders minimize tracking error against signal prices but incur elevated market impact during the closing auction for less-liquid instruments. For a $100k book with max $4,000 per trade, slippage at the open the following day is smaller in absolute dollar terms than the model risk of overnight gap exposure. The optimal execution window is asset-class-dependent:

- **Liquid US equity ETFs** (SPY, QQQ, IWM, DIA): MOC is optimal. Closing auction liquidity for these names exceeds $1B daily; our $1-4k trades are noise. Expected slippage: less than 1 bp.
- **Sector ETFs** (XLK, XLF, XLE, etc.): MOC is acceptable but slippage rises to 2-5 bp for the least liquid names (XLRE, XLB). TWAP over final 30 minutes may reduce impact by 1-2 bp.
- **International equity ETFs** (VGK, EEM, EFA): Next-open is preferred because the US close occurs hours after the underlying markets close, creating stale NAV risk. Opening prices better reflect overnight information from foreign markets.
- **Fixed income ETFs** (TLT, IEF, SHY, LQD, HYG, TIP): MOC is acceptable. Bond ETF creation/redemption keeps spreads tight. Exception: HYG in stress regimes (VIX > 25) where bid-ask widens materially.
- **Crypto** (BTC-USD, ETH-USD, SOL-USD, XRP-USD, ADA-USD): No meaningful "close." Optimal reference price is the 00:00 UTC candle close, aligning with the start of the Asian session. Executing within a 1-hour TWAP around this reference reduces timing noise.
- **Forex** (EURUSD=X, GBPUSD=X, etc.): WM/Reuters 4:00 PM London fix is the natural reference, but our signals are generated off the Yahoo Finance daily bar. Execution at the New York open (8:00 AM ET) provides the deepest liquidity for major pairs.

**Magnitude prediction**: Across the full portfolio, switching from a naive close-price fill to asset-class-optimized execution saves 3-8 bp per trade on a weighted average basis, translating to approximately 30-80 bp annually for a portfolio turning over 1x per month.

**Regime dependence**: In high-vol regimes (VIX > 25), the cost of next-open execution rises by 5-15 bp due to gap risk, shifting the balance toward MOC even for less-liquid names.

## Methodology

### Phase 1: Data Collection (Weeks 1-2)

1. **Download minute-bar data** for the 17 US equity ETFs from a provider offering historical intraday data (Polygon.io, FirstRate Data, or Alpha Vantage). Minimum 2 years of 1-minute bars. Required fields: timestamp, open, high, low, close, volume.
2. **Extract closing auction metrics**: For each ETF, isolate volume and price in the final 10 minutes (3:50-4:00 PM ET) versus the rest of the trading day. Compute the ratio of closing-auction volume to total daily volume.
3. **Compute overnight gaps**: For each instrument, calculate `open_t+1 / close_t - 1` for every trading day. Build distributions by asset class and VIX regime.
4. **Fetch hourly OHLCV** for crypto (from Binance or CoinGecko API) and forex (from OANDA or Yahoo Finance hourly) over the same period.
5. **Merge VIX daily close** from FRED or Yahoo Finance (symbol `^VIX`) to every observation for regime conditioning.

### Phase 2: Spread Estimation (Weeks 2-3)

1. **Roll (1984) spread estimator**: Compute the effective spread from the autocovariance of daily returns for each instrument. Formula: `Spread = 2 * sqrt(-Cov(r_t, r_{t-1}))` when the covariance is negative, else zero.
2. **Corwin & Schultz (2012) high-low spread estimator**: Estimate daily bid-ask spreads from consecutive daily high and low prices. This requires only OHLC data already stored in the system's DuckDB (`market_data_daily` table).
3. **Cross-validate** Roll and Corwin-Schultz estimates against actual bid-ask data for the subset of instruments where NBBO data is available (e.g., from IEX Cloud for US equities).
4. **Build a spread panel**: `{symbol, date, roll_spread, cs_spread, vix, regime}` in a Polars DataFrame.

### Phase 3: Execution Cost Simulation (Weeks 3-4)

1. **Simulate three execution strategies** for each historical signal:
   - **MOC**: Fill at the official closing price (4:00 PM bar close).
   - **TWAP-30**: Average price of the last 30 minutes, computed from minute bars as `mean(close_3:30pm_to_4:00pm)`.
   - **Next-Open**: Fill at the next trading day's opening price.
2. **Compute execution cost** relative to the signal price (the close used by the indicator engine):
   - `cost_bps = (fill_price / signal_price - 1) * 10000`
3. **Condition on VIX regime**: Partition all observations into low-vol (VIX < 15), normal (15-25), and stress (VIX > 25). Report median, mean, standard deviation, and 95th percentile of execution cost within each partition.
4. **Statistical tests**: Run Kruskal-Wallis tests across asset classes within each regime to determine if execution cost differences are statistically significant (p < 0.05).

### Phase 4: Portfolio-Level Impact (Week 4)

1. **Replay historical trades**: Using the system's historical trade log (from the `trade_log` table in DuckDB), re-price each executed trade under all three execution strategies.
2. **Compute portfolio-level P&L difference**: What would aggregate P&L have been under MOC vs. TWAP-30 vs. Next-Open?
3. **Decompose by asset class**: Which asset classes contribute most to the execution cost differential?
4. **Sensitivity analysis**: Vary the TWAP window (15, 30, 60 minutes) and measure the effect on cost.

## Literature Review

### Almgren & Chriss (2000)

- **Full citation**: Almgren, R. & Chriss, N. (2000). "Optimal Execution of Portfolio Transactions." *Journal of Risk*, 3(2), 5-39.
- **Relevance**: Foundational framework for optimal execution under the tradeoff between market impact (which favors slower execution) and timing risk (which favors faster execution). Introduces the "efficient frontier" of execution strategies parameterized by risk aversion.
- **What to extract**: The permanent vs. temporary impact model. For our $100k book, permanent impact is negligible -- our orders are far below the threshold where they move prices. This simplifies the problem: we care about timing risk (gap exposure) and bid-ask cost, not about the Almgren-Chriss impact cost. Confirm this by computing our order size as a fraction of ADV for each instrument.

### Kissell & Malamut (2005)

- **Full citation**: Kissell, R. & Malamut, R. (2005). "Algorithmic Decision Making Framework." *Journal of Trading*, 1(1), 12-21.
- **Relevance**: Provides the I-Star model for estimating market impact as a function of order size, volatility, and market participation rate. Includes practical cost estimates for institutional-size orders.
- **What to extract**: The participation rate concept. Our orders at $1-4k notional represent participation rates well below 0.01% for liquid ETFs. The framework predicts negligible impact for such sizes. For ADA-USD or XLRE, participation rates may reach 0.1-0.5% during low-volume periods. Use their calibrated parameters to estimate the dollar impact.

### Cushing & Madhavan (2000)

- **Full citation**: Cushing, D. & Madhavan, A. (2000). "Stock Returns and Trading at the Close." *Journal of Financial Markets*, 3(1), 45-67.
- **Relevance**: Documents the price dynamics of the closing auction, including the "marking the close" effect and return reversal patterns. Shows that stocks in indices (relevant for our ETF universe) exhibit distinct closing-auction behavior due to passive fund rebalancing.
- **What to extract**: The closing-period return premium/discount by day of week and index membership. Quantifies how much of the daily return is realized in the final minutes. Relevant for deciding whether MOC captures or costs alpha.

### Barclay & Hendershott (2003)

- **Full citation**: Barclay, M.J. & Hendershott, T. (2003). "Price Discovery and Trading After Hours." *Review of Financial Studies*, 16(4), 1041-1073.
- **Relevance**: Analyzes information content of after-hours and pre-market trading. Demonstrates that overnight returns contain meaningful information, especially for instruments with international exposure.
- **What to extract**: The fraction of daily price variance attributable to overnight vs. intraday trading. For international ETFs (VGK, EEM, EFA), overnight returns should dominate because the underlying markets trade during our overnight. This supports the hypothesis that next-open is better than MOC for international names.

### Additional References

- **Bogousslavsky (2016)**: "Infrequent Rebalancing, Return Autocorrelation, and Seasonality." *Journal of Finance*, 71(6). Documents intraday patterns driven by fund rebalancing -- relevant to whether MOC timing introduces systematic bias.
- **Hasbrouck (2009)**: "Trading Costs and Returns for US Equities: Estimating Effective Costs from Daily Data." *Journal of Finance*, 64(3). Provides benchmark effective-cost estimates from daily data using Bayesian methods.

## Evaluation Criteria

### Success Thresholds

| Metric | Threshold | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Cost difference between strategies | Statistically significant at p < 0.05 in at least 3 of 6 asset classes | If no asset class shows a significant difference, execution timing does not matter at our size. |
| Dollar magnitude | Aggregate annual cost savings > $50 (5 bp on $100k) | Below this, the complexity of implementation outweighs the benefit. |
| Regime dependence | Regime coefficient significant in a regression of execution cost on VIX bucket | If regime does not matter, we can use a single static policy. |
| Spread estimator validity | Roll and CS estimates within 2x of actual bid-ask for US equity ETFs where we have NBBO data | If estimators are wildly off, we cannot trust them for less-liquid asset classes. |

### Failure Conditions

- **If cost differences are below 1 bp across all asset classes**: Conclude that execution timing is immaterial at $100k and recommend no changes. Document the finding and close.
- **If overnight gap risk dominates**: The research may recommend MOC universally, simplifying the system.
- **If intraday data is unavailable or too expensive**: Fall back to daily-only analysis using Roll/CS spreads and overnight gaps, which can be computed from existing OHLCV data in DuckDB.

## Anti-Overfitting Warnings

### Specific Pitfalls for This Research

1. **Survivorship bias in ETF data**: Some sector ETFs (XLRE, XLC) were launched after 2015. Ensure analysis windows match instrument availability. Do not extrapolate XLRE results to periods before its inception (June 2015).

2. **Look-ahead bias in VIX regime conditioning**: When conditioning execution cost on VIX regime, use the **prior day's** VIX close to classify regime -- not the same day's. The system generates signals at EOD and would need to choose execution strategy before the current day's VIX is known.

3. **Data snooping via parameter optimization**: If you optimize the TWAP window (e.g., testing 15, 20, 25, 30, 45, 60 minutes), report results for all windows, not just the best one. The risk of p-hacking increases with the number of windows tested.

4. **Transaction cost asymmetry**: Slippage is not symmetric for buys and sells. Buying at the close incurs a different cost profile than selling at the close (due to index rebalancing flows). Analyze buys and sells separately.

5. **Small-sample risk in high-VIX regime**: VIX > 25 occurs roughly 15% of trading days. With 500 days of data, that is only ~75 observations per instrument in the stress regime. Report confidence intervals, not just point estimates.

6. **Crypto market structure changes**: Crypto microstructure has changed dramatically since 2020 (institutional adoption, ETF launches, exchange consolidation). Results from pre-2022 may not generalize. Consider using only the most recent 12-18 months of crypto data.

7. **Forex fix manipulation literature**: The WM/Reuters 4 PM London fix has documented manipulation cases (Barclays et al., 2013-2015). Be cautious about using fix prices as a benchmark for forex without acknowledging this context.

8. **Neglecting the null hypothesis**: The most likely outcome for a $100k book is that execution timing does not matter because our orders are too small to cause market impact. Design the research to efficiently confirm or reject this null hypothesis before building elaborate models.
