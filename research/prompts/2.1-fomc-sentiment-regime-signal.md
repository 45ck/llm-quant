## System Context

This research brief targets **llm-quant**, a systematic macro trading system managing a $100k paper trading portfolio across 39 tradeable assets spanning six asset classes: US equity ETFs (SPY, QQQ, IWM, DIA, VTI, IVV, XLK, XLF, XLE, XLV, XLI, XLY, XLP, XLU, XLRE, XLB, XLC), international equity (VGK, EEM, EFA), fixed income (TLT, IEF, SHY, LQD, HYG, TIP), commodities (GLD, SLV, USO), crypto (BTC-USD, ETH-USD, SOL-USD, XRP-USD, ADA-USD), and forex (EURUSD=X, GBPUSD=X, USDJPY=X, AUDUSD=X, USDCHF=X).

**Technology stack**: Python 3.12, Polars DataFrames, DuckDB for persistent storage, Pydantic for config validation, Jinja2 prompt templates.

**Current indicators** (computed in `src/llm_quant/data/indicators.py`): SMA(20/50/200), Bollinger Bands (20-period, 2 std), RSI(14) via Wilder's smoothing, MACD(12,26,9), ATR(14). All computed per-symbol via `group_by`/`over` partitioning in pure Polars expressions.

**Current regime classification** (in `src/llm_quant/brain/context.py`): Uses two inputs -- VIX level (fetched via `_get_vix()` from `^VIX` or `VIX` market data) and a yield spread proxy computed as `(TLT/SHY - 1.0) * 100` via `_get_yield_spread()`. SPY trend is determined by SMA50 vs SMA200 crossover via `_get_spy_trend()`. The `MarketContext` dataclass (in `src/llm_quant/brain/models.py`) carries `vix: float`, `yield_spread: float`, and `spy_trend: str` but has no field for Fed sentiment.

**Risk framework** (in `src/llm_quant/risk/limits.py` and `src/llm_quant/risk/manager.py`): 8 pre-trade checks -- position size (max 4% NAV/trade per `config/risk.toml`), position weight (max 12%, 5% crypto, 8% forex), gross exposure (max 200%), net exposure (max 100%), sector concentration (max 30%), cash reserve (min 5%), stop-loss required on every position, and drawdown circuit breaker (max 15% drawdown).

**Pipeline**: `scripts/build_context.py` fetches data if stale, computes indicators, builds `MarketContext`, renders prompts, outputs JSON. Claude LLM reads context and produces a `TradingDecision` with `MarketRegime` enum (`risk_on`/`risk_off`/`transition`), up to 5 `TradeSignal` objects. `scripts/execute_decision.py` runs risk checks via `RiskManager.filter_signals()` and executes approved trades.

**Targets**: Sharpe > 0.8, max drawdown < 15%, Sortino > 1.0, Calmar > 0.5. Primary benchmark: 60/40 SPY/TLT.

## Research Question

Can NLP-extracted sentiment from FOMC statements, minutes, and Fed speeches improve regime classification beyond VIX + yield curve slope, and does that improved classification produce better entry/exit timing on rate-sensitive assets TLT, GLD, and SPY?

Specifically:

1. Can an LLM extract a continuous hawkish/dovish "policy stance" score from Fed communications that is meaningfully different from what the yield curve already reflects?
2. Does adding this score to the regime classifier reduce false regime transitions (i.e., whipsaws where the system flips between risk_on and risk_off within a short window)?
3. Does the improved regime classification translate into higher risk-adjusted returns on TLT, GLD, and SPY positions?

---

## Hypothesis

FOMC communication carries hawkish/dovish tone shifts that appear in language days to weeks before they are fully reflected in yield curve movements. The yield curve is a lagging signal of monetary policy intent -- it reprices after the market digests Fed communication, not simultaneously with the communication itself.

**Falsifiable prediction**: An LLM-derived "Fed policy stance" score, ranging from -1.0 (maximum dovish) to +1.0 (maximum hawkish), when added as a third input to the regime classifier alongside VIX and yield spread:

- Reduces false regime transitions by at least 20% (measured as round-trip regime flips within 10 trading days that revert to the original regime).
- Improves regime classification F1-score by at least 0.05 when evaluated against a labeled ground truth of risk_on/risk_off periods (defined post-hoc by forward 20-day SPY returns: >2% = risk_on, <-2% = risk_off, else transition).
- Improves Sharpe ratio on a TLT-only momentum strategy by at least 0.10 (regime-filtered entries vs. non-regime-filtered baseline).
- Generates a signal with at least 2-day lead time over yield curve slope changes.

**Expected magnitude**: The improvement should be marginal but consistent. Fed sentiment is a refinement signal, not a standalone alpha source. If the improvement exceeds Sharpe +0.30, suspect overfitting.

## Methodology

### Phase 1: Data Collection and Corpus Construction (Week 1-2)

1. **Scrape FOMC statements** from the Federal Reserve website (https://www.federalreserve.gov/monetarypolicy/fomccalendars.htm). Collect all statements from 2000-present. These are HTML pages with consistent structure.
2. **Scrape FOMC minutes** (released 3 weeks after each meeting). Same source. Minutes are longer and contain more nuanced language about policy debates.
3. **Scrape Fed speeches** from the Board of Governors speeches page. Filter to speeches by FOMC voting members only. Tag each speech with the speaker's known hawkish/dovish lean.
4. **Normalize text**: Strip HTML, extract clean text paragraphs, tag with date, document type (statement/minutes/speech), and speaker where applicable.
5. **Store in DuckDB**: Create a `fed_communications` table with columns: `doc_id`, `date`, `doc_type`, `speaker`, `text`, `word_count`.

### Phase 2: Sentiment Scoring Engine (Week 2-3)

1. **Dictionary-based baseline**: Apply the Loughran-McDonald financial sentiment dictionary. Compute `(positive_words - negative_words) / total_words` as a raw score. This is the benchmark NLP model to beat.
2. **Domain-specific dictionary**: Extend L-M with a custom Fed vocabulary: "accommodate/accommodative" = dovish, "taper/tapering" = hawkish, "patient/patience" = dovish, "normalize/normalization" = hawkish. Build this from the Hansen et al. (2018) analysis.
3. **LLM-based scoring**: Use a local LLM (or API) to score each document on a -1.0 to +1.0 hawkish/dovish scale. Design a prompt that asks:
   - "Rate the following Federal Reserve communication on a scale from -1.0 (extremely dovish, favoring lower rates, more accommodation) to +1.0 (extremely hawkish, favoring higher rates, tighter policy). Respond with only a number."
   - Test with 3 different prompt formulations and measure inter-prompt agreement.
4. **Temporal aggregation**: Compute a rolling "Fed tone" time series:
   - On FOMC statement days: replace tone with statement score (highest signal-to-noise).
   - On minutes release days: blend 0.7 * current_tone + 0.3 * minutes_score.
   - On speech days: blend 0.9 * current_tone + 0.1 * speech_score.
   - On non-event days: carry forward the most recent score with exponential decay (half-life = 15 trading days).
5. **Validation**: Compare LLM scores against known policy pivots (2013 taper tantrum, 2018 "patient" pivot, 2020 emergency cuts, 2022 hiking cycle). Manual review of at least 20 documents to verify face validity.

### Phase 3: Regime Classifier Enhancement (Week 3-4)

1. **Current baseline**: Implement the existing regime logic (VIX + yield spread + SPY trend) as a formal model with discrete outputs. Compute a confusion matrix against labeled periods.
2. **Enhanced classifier**: Add the Fed tone score as a third continuous input. Test three approaches:
   - **Rule-based**: If Fed tone < -0.3 AND VIX < 20, bias toward risk_on. If Fed tone > 0.3 AND VIX > 25, bias toward risk_off.
   - **Logistic regression**: Train a 3-class logistic regression on {VIX, yield_spread, fed_tone} -> {risk_on, risk_off, transition}. Use walk-forward validation with 2-year training windows.
   - **Gradient-boosted trees**: XGBoost with the same features. More expressive but higher overfit risk.
3. **Regime transition analysis**: Count false transitions (regime changes that revert within 10 days) under baseline vs. enhanced classifier.
4. **Statistical significance**: Use McNemar's test to compare classification accuracy between baseline and enhanced. Require p < 0.05.

### Phase 4: Trading Strategy Backtest (Week 4-5)

1. **TLT regime-filtered strategy**: Buy TLT when regime = risk_off AND Fed tone < -0.2 (dovish shift expected to push rates lower). Sell when regime transitions to risk_on OR Fed tone > +0.2. Compare Sharpe/drawdown vs. unfiltered momentum.
2. **GLD regime-filtered strategy**: Buy GLD when regime = risk_off AND Fed tone shifting dovish (rate expectations falling, positive for gold). Compare against GLD buy-and-hold.
3. **SPY entry timing**: Use Fed tone to filter SPY entries -- delay buys if Fed tone is hawkish and rising (tightening cycle). Compare entry quality (average forward 20-day returns of entries).
4. **Full portfolio integration**: Run the complete llm-quant pipeline with and without Fed tone in the context. Compare portfolio-level Sharpe, Sortino, max drawdown, and Calmar.

### Phase 5: Robustness Testing (Week 5-6)

1. **Out-of-sample**: Train on 2000-2018, test on 2019-2024. Then train on 2000-2020, test on 2021-2024.
2. **Parameter sensitivity**: Vary the exponential decay half-life (10, 15, 20, 30 days), blending weights, and tone thresholds. Ensure results are not concentrated in a narrow parameter band.
3. **Regime lag analysis**: Measure how many days the Fed tone score leads or lags yield curve movements. The hypothesis requires at least 2-day lead.

## Literature Review

### Loughran & McDonald (2011), "When is a Liability not a Liability? Textual Analysis, Dictionaries, and 10-Ks"
- **Journal**: Journal of Finance, 66(1), 35-65
- **Key finding**: General-purpose sentiment dictionaries (like Harvard General Inquirer) perform poorly on financial text. Words like "liability," "tax," and "capital" have neutral or positive financial meaning but are tagged negative in general dictionaries.
- **Extract for this research**: Use the Loughran-McDonald (L-M) financial sentiment dictionary as the baseline NLP model. Download from https://sraf.nd.edu/loughranmcdonald-master-dictionary/. This dictionary contains ~2,700 negative, ~354 positive, ~297 uncertainty, ~19 litigious, ~184 constraining, and ~56 superfluous word lists specific to financial text.
- **Implication**: The L-M dictionary is better than general sentiment for financial text but was designed for 10-K filings, not monetary policy. Fed-specific vocabulary (e.g., "accommodate," "taper," "patient") is underrepresented. Our LLM approach should outperform L-M on Fed text.

### Hansen, McMahon & Prat (2018), "Transparency and Deliberation Within the FOMC: A Computational Linguistics Approach"
- **Journal**: Quarterly Journal of Economics, 133(2), 801-870
- **Key finding**: Applied Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) topic modeling to FOMC transcripts. Found that deliberation quality declined after 1993 when transcripts became public -- members engaged in less genuine debate and more prepared statements. Identified distinct "hawk" and "dove" topics.
- **Extract for this research**: Their topic distributions provide a validated basis for what hawkish vs. dovish language looks like in FOMC context. Use their identified topic words to validate our LLM scoring. Also use their finding that *minutes* (published) differ from *transcripts* (released with a 5-year lag) -- our real-time system can only use minutes and statements.
- **Implication**: If LDA topics can distinguish hawk/dove leanings, an LLM should perform at least as well, with the advantage of understanding context and negation.

### Cieslak & Schrimpf (2019), "Non-Monetary News in Central Bank Communication"
- **Journal**: Journal of International Economics, 118, 293-315
- **Key finding**: Decomposed Fed communications into monetary policy news and non-monetary (economic outlook) news. Found that non-monetary news dominates market reactions. Markets respond to the Fed's *assessment of the economy* more than to explicit rate guidance.
- **Extract for this research**: Our sentiment scorer should capture both dimensions -- explicit rate guidance (easy to detect) AND economic outlook tone (harder, requires understanding of "growth risks" vs. "solid expansion" language). The LLM prompt should explicitly ask for both dimensions.
- **Implication**: A single hawkish/dovish score may be too coarse. Consider a 2D score: (rate_direction, growth_outlook). However, start with 1D for simplicity and only add complexity if the 1D version shows promise.

### Gorodnichenko & Weber (2016), "Are Sticky Prices Costly? Evidence from the Stock Market"
- **Journal**: American Economic Review, 106(1), 165-199
- **Key finding**: Monetary policy surprises have heterogeneous effects across industries. Sectors with stickier prices respond more to surprise rate changes. Technology and durable goods sectors are most rate-sensitive.
- **Extract for this research**: Informs which assets in our universe should respond most to Fed sentiment shifts. TLT (directly), GLD (inversely correlated with real rates), XLK and XLY (rate-sensitive sectors), XLRE (highly rate-sensitive). Use these as the primary evaluation assets for the Fed tone signal.
- **Implication**: The signal should have differential effects across our 39-asset universe. Don't expect it to help with USO or EEM positioning.

### Baker, Bloom & Davis (2016), "Measuring Economic Policy Uncertainty"
- **Journal**: Quarterly Journal of Economics, 131(4), 1593-1636
- **Key finding**: Constructed the Economic Policy Uncertainty (EPU) index from newspaper coverage, tax code expirations, and economic forecaster disagreement. EPU spikes correlate with lower investment, employment, and output.
- **Extract for this research**: The EPU index is a useful external validator for our Fed tone score. When Fed tone shifts hawkish, EPU should rise (more uncertainty about tightening impact). Use EPU as a sanity check, not as a direct input. Download from https://www.policyuncertainty.com/. Monthly frequency, freely available.
- **Implication**: If our Fed tone score does not correlate with EPU during known policy uncertainty episodes, something is wrong with the scorer.

### Integration Architecture

The Fed tone score enters the system at the `MarketContext` level, not the indicator level. It is a single scalar value representing the current monetary policy stance. The LLM brain already processes `vix`, `yield_spread`, and `spy_trend` as macro context -- `fed_tone` would be a fourth macro input on the same level.

The `RiskManager` does not need modification. Fed tone affects *signal generation* (which trades the LLM proposes) but not *risk checking* (which trades pass the limits).

- If max drawdown worsens by more than 2 percentage points in any test, reject regardless of Sharpe improvement.

## Anti-Overfitting Warnings

### Specific Pitfalls for This Research

1. **Look-ahead bias in minutes scoring**: FOMC minutes are released 3 weeks after the meeting. Do not use the minutes score until the release date, not the meeting date. This is the single most common error in Fed NLP research.

2. **Survivorship bias in speech selection**: Only score speeches that were publicly available on the day they were delivered. Some speeches are revised or posted with a delay.

3. **Regime label contamination**: The "ground truth" regime labels (based on forward 20-day SPY returns) use future information. This is acceptable for *training evaluation* but the backtest itself must be strictly causal. Never use forward returns as an input to the trading strategy.

4. **Data snooping via parameter tuning**: The exponential decay half-life, blending weights, and tone thresholds represent 5+ degrees of freedom. With only ~200 FOMC events over 25 years, it is easy to overfit. Use walk-forward validation exclusively; no full-sample optimization.

5. **HARKing (Hypothesizing After Results Known)**: Define the hypothesis (Fed tone leads yield curve by 2+ days, F1 improvement >= 0.05) before running any tests. Do not adjust thresholds after seeing results. Record the hypothesis in git before running the first backtest.

6. **Structural breaks in Fed communication**: The Fed's communication strategy has changed dramatically. Before 2011, there were no regular press conferences. Forward guidance language ("considerable time," "patient") was introduced in 2009. The 2020 average inflation targeting framework changed the vocabulary. A model trained on 2000-2015 data may not work on 2020-2024 data. Test across sub-periods.

7. **Small sample size**: With 8 FOMC meetings per year, you have only ~200 statement events over 25 years. This is a small sample for any statistical test. Be cautious about claiming significance. Use the speeches and minutes to augment the sample, but weight the analysis toward out-of-sample performance.

8. **LLM scoring instability**: LLM outputs may not be perfectly deterministic. Run each document through the scorer 3 times and check variance. If the standard deviation of scores for a single document exceeds 0.15 (on the -1 to +1 scale), the scorer needs refinement.

9. **Confounding with VIX**: Fed communications often coincide with VIX movements (markets react to the same news the Fed is discussing). Ensure the Fed tone signal provides incremental information *beyond* VIX, not just a correlated proxy. Partial correlation analysis (Fed tone | VIX, yield spread) is required.

10. **Benchmark contamination**: The 60/40 SPY/TLT benchmark is directly affected by Fed policy. An improved regime classifier that helps TLT timing will mechanically outperform the benchmark. Report alpha relative to buy-and-hold TLT as well, not just vs. 60/40.
