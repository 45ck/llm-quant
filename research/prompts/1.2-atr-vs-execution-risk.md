# 1.2 ATR as an Execution Risk Proxy: Regime-Dependent Sizing Bias

> **Domain**: Market Microstructure & Execution | **Priority**: P1 | **Status**: Pending

## System Context

This research targets **llm-quant**, a systematic macro trading system managing a $100,000 paper trading portfolio across 39 instruments in six asset classes: US equity ETFs (SPY, QQQ, IWM, DIA, VTI, IVV, XLK, XLF, XLE, XLV, XLI, XLY, XLP, XLU, XLRE, XLB, XLC), international equity (VGK, EEM, EFA), fixed income (TLT, IEF, SHY, LQD, HYG, TIP), commodities (GLD, SLV, USO), crypto (BTC-USD, ETH-USD, SOL-USD, XRP-USD, ADA-USD), and forex (EURUSD=X, GBPUSD=X, USDJPY=X, AUDUSD=X, USDCHF=X).

**Current ATR implementation** in `src/llm_quant/data/indicators.py` (`_compute_atr()`, lines 237-264): Wilder's smoothing with `alpha = 1/14` over True Range `= max(H-L, |H-prev_close|, |L-prev_close|)`. ATR is surfaced to the LLM via `build_market_context()` in `src/llm_quant/brain/context.py` as the `atr_14` field in `MarketRow`. The LLM uses ATR to gauge volatility for position sizing and stop-loss placement.

**Risk architecture**: 8 pre-trade checks in `src/llm_quant/risk/limits.py` enforce hard limits -- max 4% NAV per trade, 12% per position (5% crypto, 8% forex), 200% gross exposure, 100% net exposure, 30% sector concentration, 5% cash reserve, stop-loss required, and a 15% drawdown circuit breaker (triggers at 12% with 3% buffer). Parameters in `config/risk.toml`. Stop-loss defaults: 5% equities, 15% crypto, 3% forex.

**Current gap**: ATR(14) is a backward-looking measure with a 14-day half-life (~9.55 days to reflect 50% of a regime change, ~22 days for 80%). During rapid regime transitions (VIX jumping from 15 to 35 in a week), ATR lags actual conditions by 5-10 days. Two failure modes emerge:

1. **Low-to-high-vol transition**: ATR understates realized volatility. The system sizes positions too large relative to actual conditions, and stop-losses set as ATR multiples are too tight (triggering premature exits).
2. **High-to-low-vol transition**: ATR overstates realized volatility. The system sizes positions too small, missing alpha, and stop-losses are too wide (allowing excessive drawdown before exiting).

Neither ATR nor any current indicator measures **execution risk** -- the bid-ask spread cost of getting filled near the signal price. Execution risk depends on market depth, adverse selection, and order flow, none of which are captured by a trailing price-range metric.

---

## Research Question

Does ATR(14) adequately proxy execution risk across VIX regimes, or does it systematically undersize positions in low-volatility environments and oversize in stress regimes? Specifically:

1. What is the correlation between ATR(14) and realized effective bid-ask spreads (Roll and Corwin-Schultz estimators) across the 39-instrument universe?
2. During regime transitions (VIX moving >5 points in 5 trading days), how long does ATR take to reflect the new regime's execution costs?
3. Would a hybrid sizing model incorporating VIX directly reduce large fills during high-spread environments?
4. What is the net Sharpe improvement from regime-adjusted sizing versus ATR-only sizing?

---

## Hypothesis

ATR is a lagging and incomplete proxy for execution risk. It captures historical price range but misses the bid-ask spread component. This creates systematic sizing errors during regime transitions:

- **Low-vol regimes** (VIX < 15): ATR reflects small daily ranges correctly, but effective spreads for less-liquid instruments (ADA-USD, XLRE, HYG) may be disproportionately large relative to ATR. The execution risk premium is undersized.
- **Regime transitions** (VIX +5 in 5 days): ATR takes 7-12 trading days to converge to within 20% of the new realized TR. During this window, ATR-based sizing is stale.
- **Stress regimes** (VIX > 25): ATR eventually reflects high volatility (leading to smaller positions, which is directionally correct), but effective spreads widen even more than ATR suggests. HYG bid-ask can blow out from 5 bp to 50+ bp; small-cap crypto from 10 bp to 200+ bp.

**Magnitude prediction**: A hybrid model multiplying ATR-based size by a VIX-regime adjustment factor would reduce the standard deviation of execution cost per trade by 15-30%, improving net Sharpe by 0.05-0.15. The improvement comes primarily from avoiding oversized fills during the first week of a high-vol regime.

**Null hypothesis**: ATR is sufficient because (a) the 4% max trade size cap already limits damage from sizing errors, and (b) the Sharpe improvement from better sizing is below estimation noise for a $100k portfolio.

## Methodology

### Phase 1: Spread Estimation (Weeks 1-2)

#### Roll (1984) Spread Estimator

Infer the effective bid-ask spread from first-order autocovariance of daily log returns:

```
r_t = ln(close_t / close_{t-1})
Cov = (1/N) * sum((r_t - r_bar) * (r_{t-1} - r_bar))   for t in [2, N]
Spread_Roll = 2 * sqrt(max(0, -Cov))
Spread_bps = Spread_Roll * 10000
```

Compute for each symbol using a rolling 20-day window. The negative-covariance condition reflects bid-ask bounce: a buy at the ask followed by a sell at the bid creates negative serial correlation. Limitation: returns zero when autocovariance is positive (trending markets, which is exactly when our momentum system is most active). Set a floor of 1 bp.

#### Corwin & Schultz (2012) High-Low Estimator

Uses the insight that daily highs approximate the ask and daily lows approximate the bid:

```
beta_t = [ln(H_t/L_t)]^2 + [ln(H_{t+1}/L_{t+1})]^2
gamma_t = [ln(max(H_t,H_{t+1}) / min(L_t,L_{t+1}))]^2
alpha_t = (sqrt(2*beta) - sqrt(beta)) / (3 - 2*sqrt(2)) - sqrt(gamma / (3 - 2*sqrt(2)))
Spread_CS = 2 * (exp(alpha_t) - 1) / (1 + exp(alpha_t))
```

Negative alpha values set to zero. Advantage over Roll: works in trending markets. Disadvantage: noisy for assets with infrequent overnight gaps (crypto, which trades 24/7 and has small close-to-open gaps).

#### Validation and Panel Construction

Cross-validate both estimators against actual NBBO bid-ask data from IEX Cloud for the subset of US equity ETFs. Build spread panel as a Polars DataFrame:

```
symbol | date | asset_class | atr_14 | roll_spread_bps | cs_spread_bps | conservative_spread_bps | vix_close | vix_regime | spread_to_atr_ratio
```

Where `conservative_spread_bps = max(roll_spread_bps, cs_spread_bps)` and `spread_to_atr_ratio = conservative_spread / atr_14`. A stable ratio across regimes supports ATR as a good proxy; a rising ratio in stress regimes confirms the hypothesis.

### Phase 2: ATR Lag Analysis (Weeks 2-3)

1. **Identify regime transitions**: Find all dates where VIX moves more than 5 points in a trailing 5-day window. Classify as "entering stress" (VIX rising) or "leaving stress" (VIX falling).

2. **Track ATR convergence**: For each transition, compute `ATR_lag_ratio = ATR_14(t) / TR_realized(t)` for days 1-20 after the transition. A ratio below 1.0 during "entering stress" means ATR understates current volatility. Plot the convergence curve.

3. **Compute sizing gap**: Using the 4% max-trade-size rule, estimate the notional position size that ATR-based sizing would suggest vs. what current realized TR would suggest. Report the gap in dollars -- this quantifies the "oversizing error" during transitions.

4. **Correlation analysis**: Compute rolling 60-day rank correlations between:
   - ATR(14) and Roll spread
   - ATR(14) and CS spread
   - VIX and Roll spread
   - VIX and CS spread
   If VIX correlates more strongly with spreads than ATR does, VIX adds information value for sizing decisions.

### Phase 3: Sizing Model Comparison (Weeks 3-4)

Compare three position sizing models in a backtest over 2+ years:

**Model A -- ATR-Only (Current Baseline)**:
```
position_size = risk_budget / (atr_14 * stop_loss_multiplier)
```

**Model B -- VIX-Adjusted ATR**:
```
vix_scalar = vix_percentile_90d     # 0-1 scale, percentile of VIX over trailing 90 days
regime_adj = 1.0 - 0.5 * vix_scalar # Reduces size by up to 50% at extreme VIX
position_size = (risk_budget / (atr_14 * multiplier)) * regime_adj
```

**Model C -- Spread-Adjusted ATR**:
```
execution_cost = max(roll_spread, cs_spread)
adjusted_atr = atr_14 + execution_cost
position_size = risk_budget / (adjusted_atr * multiplier)
```

**Backtest design**: All 39 instruments. Signal source: SMA(20/50) crossover + RSI(14) overbought/oversold (simplified proxy for the system's actual signals). For each historical signal, compute position size under all three models. Apply the system's risk checks (4% max trade, position weight limits) as a post-filter. Fill at next-day close. Aggregate into portfolio-level metrics by asset class and VIX regime.

### Phase 4: Decision Table Construction (Week 4)

Synthesize findings into an actionable table the LLM can reference:

| VIX Regime | ATR Regime | Sizing Model | Stop-Loss Mult | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|

## Literature Review

**Kyle (1985)** -- "Continuous Auctions and Insider Trading." *Econometrica*, 53(6), 1315-1335. Framework for price impact as a function of order flow and information asymmetry. Introduces Kyle's lambda (market depth inverse), which varies with information asymmetry. During stress regimes, lambda rises and execution costs increase. Extract: the theoretical basis for why a pure price-range measure (ATR) is insufficient -- it captures volatility but not the spread/depth component that determines fill quality.

**Roll (1984)** -- "A Simple Implicit Measure of the Effective Bid-Ask Spread." *Journal of Finance*, 39(4), 1127-1139. Foundational daily-data spread estimator from return autocovariance. Key limitation: returns zero when autocovariance is positive (trending markets), precisely when our momentum system is most active. Extract: the formula, its assumptions (efficient market, no information asymmetry), and the practical need for a floor or complementary estimator.

**Glosten & Milgrom (1985)** -- "Bid, Ask and Transaction Prices in a Specialist Market." *Journal of Financial Economics*, 14(1), 71-100. Derives the bid-ask spread as a function of adverse selection risk. Market makers widen spreads when the probability of trading against an informed trader increases. During regime transitions, adverse selection rises, spreads widen, and ATR-based sizing becomes incomplete. Provides the economic logic for combining VIX (forward-looking fear gauge) with ATR (backward-looking range measure).

**Corwin & Schultz (2012)** -- "A Simple Way to Estimate Bid-Ask Spreads from Daily High and Low Prices." *Journal of Finance*, 67(2), 719-760. Most practical spread estimator for daily OHLCV data. Calibrated R-squared: ~0.85 for large-cap, ~0.75 for small-cap against actual NBBO. Outperforms Roll for high-volume stocks; Roll outperforms for illiquid names. For our mixed universe, use `max(Roll, CS)` as a conservative estimate.

**Additional references**: Amihud (2002), "Illiquidity and Stock Returns," *Journal of Financial Markets* -- alternative illiquidity ratio (|return|/dollar volume). Bollerslev, Li & Xue (2018), "Volume, Volatility, and Public News Announcements," *Review of Economic Studies* -- documents the decoupling of volatility and spreads around news, supporting separate volatility and liquidity measures.

## Evaluation Criteria

### Success Thresholds

| Metric | Threshold | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Spread-ATR correlation breakdown | Rank corr < 0.5 in at least one VIX regime | Confirms ATR is incomplete |
| ATR lag duration | >5 trading days to converge within 20% of realized TR | Confirms the lag hypothesis |
| Sharpe improvement | Model B or C improves Sharpe by >= 0.05 over Model A | Below 0.05, improvement is within noise |
| Execution cost reduction | Avg cost/trade decreases by >= 2 bp under best model | Meaningful for portfolio turning over 1x/month |
| Drawdown reduction | Max drawdown decreases by >= 1 percentage point | Risk-reduction is the primary objective |

### Failure Conditions

- If ATR-spread rank correlation remains above 0.7 in all regimes: ATR is sufficient. No changes recommended.
- If Sharpe improvement is below 0.03: Complexity of regime-adjusted sizing does not justify implementation. Stick with ATR-only.
- If Model C (spread-adjusted) performs worse than Model A: Spread estimators are too noisy. Fall back to Model B or stay with Model A.
- If the 4% risk cap frequently binds: Hard limits may already prevent the damage from ATR mismeasurement. Measure how often the cap binds under each model.

## Anti-Overfitting Warnings

1. **Regime definition circularity**: Use fixed VIX thresholds (15, 25) from economic reasoning (VIX median ~17, stress historically starts ~25), not optimized boundaries.
2. **VIX adjustment parameter tuning**: Model B's 0.5 cap and 90-day lookback are free parameters. Justify the 0.5 economically (prevents going to zero exposure). Test robustness with 0.3 and 0.7 alternatives.
3. **Spread estimator noise**: If day-to-day SNR < 1.0, smooth with a 5-day moving average or use regime-median rather than daily estimates before incorporating into sizing.
4. **Backtest period selection**: 2020-2024 includes extreme events (COVID crash, 2022 rate hikes). Validate on a calmer sub-period (2023-2024) to check robustness.
5. **Asymmetric sizing cost**: Reducing position size during stress reduces losses but also reduces mean-reversion alpha capture. Measure the "missed alpha" from conservative sizing in stress regimes.
6. **Multi-testing correction**: 3 models x 6 asset classes x 3 VIX regimes = 54 comparisons. Apply Bonferroni or Holm correction to avoid spurious significance.
7. **Crypto structural breaks**: Crypto microstructure changed dramatically (2020 DeFi boom, 2022 FTX collapse, 2024 BTC ETF). Weight recent data more heavily or use separate calibration windows.
8. **Confounding with order flow**: ATR and spreads are both driven by underlying order flow. The practical test is whether regime-adjusted sizing improves portfolio outcomes, regardless of whether the causal mechanism is correctly identified.

## Appendix: ATR Convergence Mathematics

Wilder's smoothing is an exponential moving average with `alpha = 1/period`:

```
ATR_t = (1 - 1/14) * ATR_{t-1} + (1/14) * TR_t

Half-life       = -ln(2) / ln(1 - 1/14)   = 9.55 days
80% convergence = -ln(0.20) / ln(13/14)    = 22 days
95% convergence = -ln(0.05) / ln(13/14)    = 41 days
```

After a regime shift, ATR reflects only 50% of the change after ~10 days, 80% after ~22 days, and 95% after ~41 days. This quantifies the window during which ATR-based sizing is systematically wrong.
