## System Context

### The llm-quant System

llm-quant is a systematic macro trading system managing a $100,000 paper trading portfolio across 39 tradeable assets spanning six asset classes:

- **US Equity ETFs (17)**: SPY, QQQ, IWM, DIA, VTI, IVV, XLK, XLF, XLE, XLV, XLI, XLY, XLP, XLU, XLRE, XLB, XLC
- **International Equity (3)**: VGK, EEM, EFA
- **Fixed Income (6)**: TLT, IEF, SHY, LQD, HYG, TIP
- **Commodities (3)**: GLD, SLV, USO
- **Crypto (5)**: BTC-USD, ETH-USD, SOL-USD, XRP-USD, ADA-USD
- **Forex (5)**: EURUSD=X, GBPUSD=X, USDJPY=X, AUDUSD=X, USDCHF=X

The system runs Python 3.12 with Polars DataFrames for computation and DuckDB for persistent storage. Technical indicators are computed in `src/llm_quant/data/indicators.py` and currently include SMA(20/50/200), Bollinger Bands(20,2), RSI(14), MACD(12,26,9), and ATR(14). Market data is stored in the `market_data_daily` table with schema `(symbol, date, open, high, low, close, volume, adj_close, sma_20, sma_50, rsi_14, macd, macd_signal, macd_hist, atr_14)`.

The decision pipeline flows: `scripts/build_context.py` (fetches data, computes indicators, assembles JSON context) -> Claude LLM (regime assessment, signal generation) -> `scripts/execute_decision.py` (risk checks, execution, persistence). Regime classification uses VIX level + TLT/SHY yield spread to categorize markets as `risk_on`, `risk_off`, or `transition` (implemented in `src/llm_quant/brain/context.py`).

Performance targets: Sharpe > 0.8, max drawdown < 15%, Sortino > 1.0, Calmar > 0.5.

### Current Risk Architecture

Seven pre-trade risk checks are implemented in `src/llm_quant/risk/limits.py` and orchestrated by `RiskManager.check_trade()` in `src/llm_quant/risk/manager.py`:

1. `check_position_size()` -- max 2% NAV per trade (`max_trade_size=0.04` in config, 0.02 in CLAUDE.md spec)
2. `check_position_weight()` -- max 10% per position (5% crypto, 8% forex)
3. `check_gross_exposure()` -- max 200% NAV
4. `check_net_exposure()` -- max 100% NAV
5. `check_sector_concentration()` -- max 30% per sector
6. `check_cash_reserve()` -- min 5% NAV
7. `check_stop_loss()` -- required on every position
8. `check_drawdown_limit()` -- blocks buys when drawdown approaches 15% (already implemented as 8th check)

Each check returns `RiskCheckResult(passed, rule, message, current_value, limit_value)`. The `RiskManager` class holds sector and asset-class mappings from the universe config, applies per-asset-class weight limits (crypto=5%, forex=8%, equity=10%), and enforces a max 5 trades per session cap via `filter_signals()`.

Risk configuration lives in `config/risk.toml` under the `[limits]` table:
```toml
max_position_weight = 0.12
max_trade_size = 0.04
max_gross_exposure = 2.0
max_net_exposure = 1.0
max_sector_concentration = 0.30
max_trades_per_session = 5
min_cash_reserve = 0.05
require_stop_loss = true
default_stop_loss_pct = 0.05
max_drawdown_pct = 0.15
crypto_max_position_weight = 0.05
crypto_default_stop_loss_pct = 0.15
forex_max_position_weight = 0.08
forex_default_stop_loss_pct = 0.03
```

### The Critical Gap This Research Addresses

**Every existing risk check operates on a single asset or on aggregate portfolio exposure. There is zero correlation awareness.** This means:

- SPY at 10% + QQQ at 10% + XLK at 10% + IWM at 8% each pass every individual check, but they behave as a ~38% leveraged equity bet during a crisis when correlations converge toward 1.0.
- The 30% sector concentration limit is a crude proxy for correlation risk, but it misses cross-sector correlation spikes (e.g., XLF and XLK become highly correlated during systemic sell-offs despite being different sectors).
- Crypto assets (BTC-USD, ETH-USD, SOL-USD, XRP-USD, ADA-USD) exhibit near-unity correlation during drawdowns; 5% each = 25% effective single-factor bet.
- The diversification benefit the portfolio assumes in calm markets **evaporates precisely when it is needed most** -- during stress events.

Without a correlation-aware pre-trade check, the system cannot distinguish between a genuinely diversified 60% invested portfolio and a concentrated 60% single-factor bet masquerading as diversification.

## Research Question

Does real-time rolling correlation monitoring with regime-conditional thresholds, enforced as a pre-trade risk check, materially reduce portfolio drawdown during correlation breakdown events without meaningfully degrading risk-adjusted returns during calm regimes?

Specifically:
1. How quickly do pairwise correlations among risk assets converge during stress events (VIX > 25)?
2. What regime-conditional correlation thresholds optimally balance drawdown protection against false-positive trade blocking?
3. Does the proposed `check_portfolio_correlation()` function, implemented as an additional pre-trade gate, reduce average drawdown depth by 20-30% during stress while preserving Sharpe in calm regimes?

## Hypothesis

During stress events (VIX > 25), pairwise correlations among risk assets converge toward 0.85-0.95 within 3-5 trading days. A dynamic correlation check that blocks new BUY trades when the average pairwise correlation between the proposed asset and existing holdings exceeds a regime-conditional threshold will:

- **Reduce average drawdown depth by 20-30%** during stress periods (defined as VIX > 25 sustained for 5+ days)
- **Reduce max drawdown by 15-25%** relative to the unconstrained portfolio
- **Preserve Sharpe ratio within 5% of unconstrained levels** during calm regimes (VIX < 20)
- **Generate fewer than 5% false-positive blocks** during normal market conditions

The thresholds hypothesized are:
- **Normal regime** (VIX < 20): block if avg correlation with existing holdings > 0.70
- **Transition regime** (VIX 20-25): block if avg correlation > 0.55
- **Stress regime** (VIX > 25): block if avg correlation > 0.45

## Methodology

### Phase 1: Correlation Surface Construction (Week 1-2)

1. **Fetch 5 years of daily close prices** for all 39 assets from the `market_data_daily` DuckDB table (or extend the fetch window via `pq fetch` if data is insufficient).

2. **Compute rolling pairwise correlation matrices** using Polars:
   - 21-day rolling window (1 month) for fast-moving correlation
   - 63-day rolling window (1 quarter) for medium-term structure
   - 252-day rolling window (1 year) for long-term baseline
   - Use log returns, not price levels, to avoid spurious correlation from common trends

3. **Store correlation surfaces** in a new DuckDB table `correlation_surface`:
   ```sql
   CREATE TABLE correlation_surface (
       date DATE NOT NULL,
       symbol_a VARCHAR NOT NULL,
       symbol_b VARCHAR NOT NULL,
       corr_21d DOUBLE,
       corr_63d DOUBLE,
       corr_252d DOUBLE,
       PRIMARY KEY (date, symbol_a, symbol_b)
   );
   ```
   This gives 39 * 38 / 2 = 741 unique pairs per date. At 5 years (~1260 trading days), approximately 934,000 rows.

4. **Classify each date into a regime** using VIX level and existing regime logic from `context.py`. Store in a `correlation_regime` table:
   ```sql
   CREATE TABLE correlation_regime (
       date DATE PRIMARY KEY,
       vix_close DOUBLE,
       regime VARCHAR NOT NULL,  -- risk_on, risk_off, transition
       avg_risk_asset_corr_21d DOUBLE,
       avg_cross_class_corr_21d DOUBLE
   );
   ```

### Phase 2: Correlation Dynamics Analysis (Week 2-3)

5. **Quantify correlation convergence speed**: For each stress onset (VIX crossing above 25 from below), measure how many days it takes for the median pairwise correlation among equity ETFs to exceed 0.80. Repeat for crypto-to-equity and commodity-to-equity pairs.

6. **Compute conditional correlation distributions**: For each regime (risk_on, risk_off, transition), compute the distribution of 21-day pairwise correlations across all 741 pairs. Report percentiles (25th, 50th, 75th, 90th, 95th) per regime.

7. **Identify correlation clusters**: Use hierarchical clustering (Ward linkage) on the average correlation matrix to identify groups of assets that behave as effective single factors. Compare cluster structure across regimes.

8. **Measure diversification ratio**: Compute the diversification ratio (weighted average volatility / portfolio volatility) under each regime. A ratio of 1.0 means no diversification benefit; higher means more diversification. Track how this ratio compresses during stress.

### Phase 3: Threshold Calibration (Week 3-4)

9. **Grid search over threshold triplets**: For each combination of (normal_threshold, transition_threshold, stress_threshold) in a reasonable grid:
   - Normal: [0.55, 0.60, 0.65, 0.70, 0.75, 0.80]
   - Transition: [0.40, 0.45, 0.50, 0.55, 0.60, 0.65]
   - Stress: [0.30, 0.35, 0.40, 0.45, 0.50, 0.55]

   Simulate the correlation check on historical portfolio decisions:
   - For each proposed BUY, compute the average 21-day correlation between the proposed asset and every existing holding (weighted by holding size)
   - Block the trade if the weighted average correlation exceeds the regime-appropriate threshold
   - Track: number of trades blocked, drawdown during subsequent 5/10/21 days, trades that would have been profitable but were blocked (false positives)

10. **Optimize thresholds** by maximizing: `drawdown_reduction * (1 - false_positive_rate)`. Select the threshold triplet that achieves the best trade-off. Validate against a held-out year of data.

### Phase 4: Implementation & Backtest (Week 4-5)

11. **Implement `compute_correlation_surface()`** in `src/llm_quant/data/indicators.py`: a function that takes a Polars DataFrame of OHLCV data and returns a 39x39 correlation matrix for a given date using the trailing 21-day window.

12. **Implement `check_portfolio_correlation()`** in `src/llm_quant/risk/limits.py` as the new pre-trade check. The function signature:
    ```python
    def check_portfolio_correlation(
        proposed_symbol: str,
        existing_positions: dict[str, float],  # symbol -> weight
        correlation_matrix: dict[tuple[str, str], float],  # (sym_a, sym_b) -> corr
        regime: str,  # "risk_on", "risk_off", "transition"
        thresholds: dict[str, float],  # regime -> max_avg_corr
    ) -> RiskCheckResult:
    ```

13. **Wire into `RiskManager.check_trade()`** in `src/llm_quant/risk/manager.py` as check #9 (after drawdown limit).

14. **Run full backtest** comparing:
    - **Baseline**: current 8-check risk system
    - **Treatment**: 9-check system with correlation limit
    - Evaluate over 5 named stress periods: COVID crash (2020-02-19 to 2020-03-23), 2022 rates shock (2022-01-03 to 2022-06-16), Crypto winter (2021-11-10 to 2022-11-21), SVB crisis (2023-03-06 to 2023-03-13), and the full 5-year period.

### Phase 5: Sensitivity & Robustness (Week 5-6)

15. **Window sensitivity**: Repeat threshold calibration with 10-day and 42-day correlation windows. Determine whether 21-day is optimal or if a blend improves results.

16. **Weight method sensitivity**: Compare unweighted average correlation, position-weight-weighted average, and risk-contribution-weighted average. The risk-contribution-weighted version should give more influence to larger positions.

17. **Transaction cost analysis**: Measure how many profitable trades the correlation filter blocks. Compute the "cost of safety" as the annualized return drag from false-positive blocking.

## Literature Review

### Core Papers

1. **Longin, F. & Solnik, B. (2001).** "Extreme Correlation of International Equity Markets." *Journal of Finance*, 56(2), 649-676.
   - **Extract**: Empirical evidence that correlations among international equity markets increase significantly during bear markets. The asymmetry is structural, not an artifact of increased volatility. Use their exceedance correlation methodology to validate our hypothesis that risk-asset correlations converge toward 0.85-0.95 during stress.
   - **Application**: Their bear-market correlation estimates provide calibration anchors for our stress-regime thresholds.

2. **Ang, A. & Chen, J. (2002).** "Asymmetric Correlations of Equity Portfolios." *Journal of Financial Economics*, 63(3), 443-494.
   - **Extract**: Downside correlations are significantly higher than upside correlations for US equities. The correlation asymmetry is economically large (0.10-0.15 higher in down markets). They provide a parametric model for conditional correlation.
   - **Application**: Use their asymmetric correlation model to justify regime-conditional thresholds. Their finding that downside correlation exceeds upside by ~0.12 supports tighter thresholds during stress.

3. **DeMiguel, V., Garlappi, L., & Uppal, R. (2009).** "Optimal Versus Naive Diversification: How Inefficient is the 1/N Portfolio?" *Review of Financial Studies*, 22(5), 1915-1953.
   - **Extract**: Estimation error in covariance matrices often overwhelms the theoretical benefits of mean-variance optimization. Equal-weight (1/N) portfolios frequently outperform optimized portfolios out-of-sample with fewer than 50 assets.
   - **Application**: Caution against over-reliance on point estimates of correlation. Use their framework to set minimum sample size requirements for our rolling correlation computation. Prefer simple threshold rules over complex optimization of the correlation constraint.

4. **Lopez de Prado, M. (2016).** "Building Diversified Portfolios that Outperform Out of Sample." *Journal of Portfolio Management*, 42(4), 59-69.
   - **Extract**: Hierarchical Risk Parity (HRP) uses hierarchical clustering on the correlation matrix to build diversified portfolios. The method is more robust to estimation error than mean-variance because it does not require matrix inversion.
   - **Application**: Use the hierarchical clustering component to identify correlation-based asset groups. The dendrogram structure reveals which assets effectively function as a single factor. This informs the correlation check by identifying hidden concentrations.

### Supplementary Papers

5. **Kritzman, M., Li, Y., Page, S., & Rigobon, R. (2011).** "Principal Components as a Measure of Systemic Risk." *Journal of Portfolio Management*, 37(4), 112-126.
   - **Extract**: The absorption ratio (fraction of total variance explained by top N principal components) rises sharply before crises. A high absorption ratio indicates correlation convergence.
   - **Application**: Consider the absorption ratio as an alternative or complementary signal to average pairwise correlation for regime detection.

6. **Engle, R. (2002).** "Dynamic Conditional Correlation: A Simple Class of Multivariate Generalized Autoregressive Conditional Heteroskedasticity Models." *Journal of Business & Economic Statistics*, 20(3), 339-350.
   - **Extract**: DCC-GARCH provides a parametric model for time-varying correlations that is more efficient than rolling windows. However, it requires numerical optimization and may be too slow for real-time pre-trade checks.
   - **Application**: Benchmark our rolling-window correlations against DCC-GARCH estimates. If rolling windows produce comparable results for our purpose (threshold-based blocking, not point estimation), prefer the simpler approach.

## Evaluation Criteria

### Success Thresholds

| Criterion | Threshold | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Avg drawdown depth reduction (stress) | >= 20% | Primary objective. Less than 20% improvement does not justify the complexity. |
| Max drawdown reduction (full period) | >= 10% | Material improvement in worst-case outcome. |
| Sharpe ratio impact (calm regimes) | <= -5% | Safety feature must not significantly impair returns during normal operation. |
| False positive rate (calm regimes) | <= 5% | More than 5% of legitimate trades blocked indicates thresholds are too tight. |
| Correlation convergence lead time | >= 2 days | The check must detect correlation increase before the drawdown fully materializes. If correlation and drawdown move simultaneously, the check has no predictive value. |
| Computation time per check | < 100ms | Must not slow down the decision pipeline. Pre-computation of the correlation surface is acceptable. |

### Failure Conditions

- If drawdown reduction is < 15% across all threshold combinations, the approach lacks sufficient signal.
- If Sharpe ratio decreases by > 10% during calm regimes, the false-positive cost is too high.
- If correlation convergence and drawdown onset are simultaneous (< 1 day lead), the check is reactive rather than predictive and provides no value as a pre-trade gate.

## Anti-Overfitting Warnings

### Specific Pitfalls for This Research

1. **Threshold snooping**: Testing many threshold triplets on the same data set and selecting the best-performing one guarantees overfitting. **Mitigation**: Use walk-forward cross-validation. Calibrate on years 1-3, validate on year 4, test on year 5. Report out-of-sample metrics only.

2. **Survivorship bias in stress events**: Only 4-5 named stress events in 5 years of data is an extremely small sample for threshold calibration. Any threshold that "works" on 4 events may be noise. **Mitigation**: Also test on randomly sampled high-VIX periods (VIX > 25 for any reason, not just named crises). Report the distribution of drawdown reductions, not just the average.

3. **Correlation =/= causation of drawdown**: High correlation among holdings does not guarantee a drawdown will occur. The portfolio could be highly correlated and all move *up*. The check should block during stress (VIX > 25) + high correlation, not high correlation alone. **Mitigation**: The regime-conditional design already addresses this, but verify that thresholds in calm regimes do not block trades that would have been profitable.

4. **Look-ahead bias in window selection**: Using a 21-day window means today's correlation estimate includes the current day's return. If today is the crash day, the correlation estimate already reflects it. **Mitigation**: Lag the correlation computation by 1 day (use data through T-1 to estimate correlation at T).

5. **Non-stationarity of correlation structure**: The "normal" correlation between SPY and QQQ may differ in 2020 vs. 2024 due to changes in market structure, index composition, or monetary policy. A fixed threshold calibrated on 2020-2022 may not apply to 2024-2025. **Mitigation**: Use relative thresholds (e.g., correlation exceeds rolling 90th percentile for this pair) instead of or in addition to absolute thresholds. Test regime-conditional absolute thresholds against relative thresholds.

6. **Dimensionality explosion**: 741 pairs with 3 windows = 2,223 features per day. Resist the temptation to build a complex model that uses all pairs as features. **Mitigation**: The proposed approach uses a single summary statistic (weighted average correlation with holdings) for the pre-trade check. Keep it simple. Complex models belong in Phase 5 sensitivity analysis, not in the production check.

7. **Crypto data quality**: Crypto assets trade 24/7 but our data is daily close. Weekend price movements create artificial correlation patterns when aligned with equity 5-day-week data. **Mitigation**: Align all data to business days. For crypto, use the close price corresponding to the equity market close (4:00 PM ET). Document any data alignment decisions.
